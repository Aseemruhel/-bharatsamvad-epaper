LEAD: The Noida International Airport at Jewar in Uttar Pradesh — known operationally by its IATA code DXN — commenced passenger flight operations on Sunday, 15 June 2026, with IndiGo as the launch carrier. The airline began with flights to Amritsar and Hyderabad on the inaugural day, expanded to Bengaluru and Jammu on 16 June, and is scheduled to extend service to over sixteen domestic destinations in the coming months. Akasa Air joins the airport on Tuesday, 16 June, with daily nonstop flights to Bengaluru and Navi Mumbai. Air India Express is also expected to commence services from the airport shortly.

The opening of DXN — the first of two newly-operational greenfield airports in India's largest metropolitan regions, the other being the Navi Mumbai International Airport which began commercial operations earlier — is the most immediately visible expression of a remarkable structural transformation underway across Indian civil aviation. Across multiple dimensions simultaneously — passenger volume growth, airport count expansion, airline fleet scaling, and the upgrading of regulatory and financial infrastructure — the trajectory of the Indian aviation sector is one of confident and sustained acceleration.

## The headline numbers

India's civil aviation sector handled approximately 350.49 million passengers during the first ten months of fiscal year 2025-26 — the period from April 2025 to January 2026 — combining domestic and international traffic. Of this total, domestic passenger traffic was 281.78 million, while international passenger traffic was 68.71 million, registering a year-on-year growth of 7.2 per cent. The international growth rate reflects the strong momentum that international travel from India has gained in the current financial year.

For the full FY 2025-26, ICRA, the credit rating agency, has projected Indian airport operators to see 18 to 20 per cent year-on-year growth, driven by rising passenger traffic, tariff revisions and growth in non-aeronautical revenue. Domestic passenger traffic alone is expected to grow 7-10 per cent year-on-year to reach 175-181 million for the full fiscal year.

The longer-term projections describe a market poised for sustained expansion at remarkable scale. The International Air Transport Association (IATA) projects India to overtake both China and the United States to become the world's third-largest air passenger market by 2030. Domestic air travel demand is expected to roughly double from current levels, reaching approximately 715 million annual domestic passengers by 2030. International traffic is projected to expand from approximately 64 million in 2023 to around 160 million by 2030 — a more than two-fold increase in just seven years.

## From 74 airports to 350: the infrastructure leap

The supporting airport infrastructure has been expanding at among the fastest paces in the world. The number of operational airports in India has more than doubled from 74 in 2014 to approximately 163 in 2026 — and the government's clearly articulated objective is to reach 350-400 operational airports by 2047, a near-tripling of current capacity over the next two decades.

Several specific expansion threads exemplify this momentum. The Noida International Airport at Jewar, which became operational on 15 June 2026, was developed under a public-private partnership model and gives the National Capital Region a third commercial airport, working alongside Indira Gandhi International Airport and Hindon Airport. The Navi Mumbai International Airport, developed by the Adani Group, creates a similarly powerful dual-airport system for the Mumbai metropolitan region.

In addition to these two greenfield facilities, the Government is moving ahead with the third phase of airport privatisation under the National Monetisation Pipeline. Eleven airports — including Amritsar, Varanasi, Bhubaneswar, Raipur and Tiruchirappalli — have been proposed for long-term leasing to private operators under the PPP model. This continues a transformational process that has, over recent years, brought world-class private operational management to several major Indian airports including Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Ahmedabad, Lucknow, Jaipur, Mangaluru, Thiruvananthapuram and Guwahati.

## The fleet: 1,700 aircraft on order

The most striking single data point describing the scale of Indian aviation's expansion is the order book of the country's airlines. Across the major Indian carriers, over 1,700 aircraft are currently on firm order — one of the largest national aircraft order books anywhere in the world. The total Indian commercial aviation fleet is projected to reach approximately 1,100 aircraft by 2027 as deliveries continue at scale.

IndiGo, the country's largest carrier and now one of the most prominent airlines globally, currently operates a fleet of more than 400 aircraft and inducted 58 new aircraft during calendar year 2024 alone. The airline operates over 2,200 daily flights, serves 90-plus domestic and 40-plus international destinations, and carried over 118 million passengers in the 2024-25 financial year — making it one of the largest carriers in the Asia-Pacific region by passenger volume. Over the past several quarters, IndiGo has doubled its Airbus A350 orders, expanding its long-haul widebody plans considerably. The airline has also launched ambitious new long-haul services to Manchester and Amsterdam Schiphol, marking a significant step into the European market.

Air India, under the Tata Group's ownership and its post-acquisition transformation programme, has placed additional substantial orders for the Boeing 737 MAX family on top of its already-significant widebody and narrowbody commitments. The Air India Group, which now includes Air India and Air India Express following the integration of Vistara, has been positioned by the Tata Group as a long-term global aviation contender with the ambition of reclaiming Air India's historic place among the world's premier carriers.

Akasa Air, the newest Indian full-service entrant, has continued an aggressive route expansion programme despite its relative youth — emerging as one of the first operators at Noida International Airport and demonstrating the depth of opportunity for new entrants in the Indian market.

## The international ascent

The international dimension of India's aviation expansion is becoming increasingly significant. India crossed the threshold of 1,000 daily international departure flights in 2022, and the figure has continued to grow strongly. IndiGo, between November 2025 and November 2026, is scaling up from 18 daily departures at the newly-opened Navi Mumbai International Airport to 140 daily departures including 30 international departures — a near-tenfold expansion at a single airport in just 12 months.

India's connectivity to the United Arab Emirates has emerged as one of the fastest-growing route bundles globally, with over 280 weekly flights connecting 20 Indian cities to five UAE destinations. The Saudi Arabia, Singapore, Thailand and Vietnam corridors have all seen substantial frequency increases.

Global airlines, recognising the opportunity, are positioning aggressively for the Indian market. Turkish Airlines is evaluating new routes between Antalya and India. The Hungary-based carrier Wizz Air has announced plans to begin Indian operations. Multiple European, East Asian and Gulf carriers have either added Indian destinations or substantially increased existing-route frequencies over the past 18 months — a clear signal of the global aviation industry's confidence in India's trajectory.

## The structural drivers

The aggregate expansion is being driven by an unusually favourable confluence of factors. The first is the underlying growth of India's economy — projected at 7.6 per cent for FY 2025-26 by the OECD, the world's fastest-growing major economy. This is producing both an expanding middle class capable of affording air travel and rising business-travel demand. Domestic passenger traffic, by industry data, is now substantively at pre-pandemic levels and continuing to grow steadily.

The second is the deliberate and effective policy push for regional connectivity through the UDAN (Ude Desh ka Aam Nagrik) scheme — now in its fifth iteration with UDAN 5.3 — which has successfully connected dozens of tier-2 and tier-3 cities to the wider aviation network for the first time. Tier-2 and tier-3 city connectivity has been a particular policy success, opening up parts of India that had remained outside the air travel ecosystem for decades.

The third is the maturation of supporting infrastructure beyond the airports themselves. The Cape Town Convention has been fully implemented in Indian law through the Protection of Interests in Aircraft Objects Act 2025, strengthening the aircraft leasing and financing framework substantially — a regulatory development of considerable importance that has improved the operating economics for Indian carriers. Reduced GST rates on unmanned aircraft (from 18-28 per cent to 5 per cent, effective September 2025) have lowered costs for the emerging drone and air taxi sectors.

The fourth is the impressive expansion of the air cargo segment, with total air freight traffic for FY26 (April-January) reaching approximately 3.29 million metric tonnes, including 1.25 MMT domestic and 2.04 MMT international. Air cargo operators including Blue Dart and Delhivery have lifted freighter utilisation to 78 per cent, supported by progressive policy steps including night-parking approvals and slot relaxations.

## Emerging frontiers

Beyond conventional passenger and cargo aviation, several exciting new segments are gaining shape. The air taxi or eVTOL category is moving rapidly through the prototype-and-feasibility stage in India, with Bengaluru-based start-up Sarala Aviation having unveiled its prototype Shunya — designed for 20-30 km urban trips at speeds up to 250 kmph, with a planned launch in 2028. Andhra Pradesh has announced ambitious plans to launch seaplane services across eight locations, opening up new tourism and connectivity possibilities.

The drone sector, supported by the GST reduction and a series of regulatory liberalisations, is expanding rapidly across both civil and commercial applications. India's drone industry is positioned to become one of the fastest-growing segments within the wider aviation ecosystem over the next three to five years.

## What it adds up to

Taken together, the expansion of operational airports from 74 to 163 with 350-400 targeted by 2047, the order book of over 1,700 aircraft across domestic carriers, the projected fleet expansion to 1,100 aircraft by 2027, the IATA projection of India becoming the world's third-largest air passenger market by 2030, the structural domestic demand projection of 715 million passengers by 2030, and the parallel build-out of supporting financial, regulatory and infrastructure frameworks, collectively describe an aviation sector entering what may be the most consequential growth decade in its history.

The aviation market size itself reflects this trajectory: from USD 14.78 billion in 2025 to a projected USD 28.96 billion by 2031, growing at a compound annual rate of 11.86 per cent — among the highest globally for an already-substantial aviation market.

## The road ahead

The immediate horizon will be defined by the operational ramp-up of the two new metropolitan airports — Navi Mumbai and Noida — as they scale toward their design capacities of 90 million and 70 million annual passengers respectively. The completion of the third-phase airport privatisation, the continued aircraft deliveries against the order book, and the progressive expansion of international route networks will define the next 18-24 months.

The longer view — toward 2030 and beyond — points to an Indian aviation sector that will, by all credible projections, occupy a substantively larger share of the global aviation economy than it does today. The 715 million domestic passenger projection for 2030, the third-largest air passenger market status, and the steady accumulation of world-class airport infrastructure across the country all describe a trajectory that places India among the most consequential aviation markets globally for the coming generation.

For now, on the morning of 16 June 2026 — with Noida airport welcoming its second carrier on its second operational day — what the Indian aviation sector has on its books is a once-in-a-generation expansion programme, in confident progress, with substantively more growth still ahead. The boom is real, it is sustained, and on every projection that international institutions, industry analysts and the airlines themselves have placed on the record, it is poised to continue defining the global aviation conversation for years to come.
