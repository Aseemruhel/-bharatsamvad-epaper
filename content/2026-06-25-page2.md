LEAD: India's emergence as a substantive global defence supplier has, in the past 72 hours, acquired one of its most consequential validation points yet. Multiple Indian government sources have confirmed — to Reuters, Business Today, India Today, and other agencies, beginning Monday, 22 June 2026 — that the United Arab Emirates and India are in early-stage but rapidly-progressing discussions for the potential sale of the BrahMos supersonic cruise missile and the Akashteer fully-automated air-defence command-and-control system. "UAE has shown interest for a number of our weapon systems including BrahMos and Akashteer. The talks between India and UAE are at initial stages and are progressing fast," one source with direct knowledge of the matter stated.

The development is consequential in multiple respects. It signals a substantive deepening of India-UAE defence cooperation — building on the comprehensive strategic partnership the two countries established in 2017 and the bilateral Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) that came into force in May 2022. It positions India as a credible alternative defence-supplier to the established American and European suppliers that have historically dominated Gulf defence procurement. It validates, in the most consequential way possible, the operational effectiveness of India's flagship indigenous defence platforms — both of which were combat-tested during Operation Sindoor, India's military response in May 2025 to the Pahalgam terror attack of April 2025. And it adds to a steadily growing list of partner nations actively considering acquisition of Indian-built defence platforms, alongside Armenia, the Philippines, and other established customers.

## What is being discussed

The two specific platforms under discussion are among the most consequential outputs of India's indigenous defence-manufacturing ecosystem.

**BrahMos** — the supersonic cruise missile jointly developed by India's Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Russia's NPO Mashinostroyenia since 1998 — is widely regarded as one of the world's fastest operational cruise missiles. The missile can be launched from land, sea, and air platforms, providing the operational flexibility that modern defence requirements demand. Multiple variants serve different mission profiles: the surface-to-surface configurations on land-mobile launchers and naval vessels; the BrahMos-A air-launched variant integrated with Indian Air Force Sukhoi-30MKI aircraft; the BrahMos-NG (next-generation) variant under continued development. The missile's combination of speed, accuracy, low reaction time for enemy defences, and operational versatility has made it among the most sought-after Indian defence exports.

**Akashteer** — developed indigenously by Bharat Electronics Limited (BEL) in collaboration with the Indian Army — is a fully-automated air-defence command-and-control system designed to integrate multiple sensors, radars, and weapons platforms into a unified air-defence network. Akashteer provides real-time tracking and engagement decision support across the spectrum of aerial threats — from cruise missiles and ballistic missiles to drones, aircraft, and helicopters. The system's value lies in its automated decision architecture, which dramatically compresses the engagement decision-cycle in defensive operations against fast-moving, multi-axis aerial threats.

The two systems together provide complementary capability. BrahMos delivers long-range strike capability across the spectrum of high-value military targets. Akashteer provides the defensive layer that protects critical assets and population centres against aerial attack. Both are products of substantial Indian engineering capability that did not exist in either category twenty years ago.

## The combat validation: Operation Sindoor

The specific reason these two platforms have acquired such substantive international interest is the operational validation they received in May 2025 during Operation Sindoor — India's military response to the 22 April 2025 Pahalgam terror attack that killed 26 Indian civilians, predominantly tourists. The operation, which involved cross-border strikes against terror infrastructure in Pakistan-administered territory, also generated retaliatory aerial responses from Pakistan that put the Indian air-defence architecture into actual combat conditions for the first time at this scale in many years.

The Akashteer system demonstrated its effectiveness in real-world conditions. Multiple Pakistani drones and missiles attempting to penetrate Indian airspace were tracked, engaged, and neutralised by the integrated air-defence network of which Akashteer is the central command-and-control element. The system's automated coordination of sensor data, threat classification, engagement decisions, and weapons-platform deployment provided the operational effectiveness that defensive doctrine requires.

The BrahMos missile, deployed in both surface-to-surface and air-launched variants, demonstrated its accuracy and operational utility in the cross-border strike phase of the operation. The combination of long range, supersonic speed, low radar cross-section, and precision targeting that the system provides was validated in operational rather than test conditions.

This combat validation is, in defence-export terms, the most consequential credential a weapons system can carry. Foreign buyers of military equipment routinely assess potential acquisitions against three criteria: technical specifications, integration compatibility, and demonstrated operational effectiveness. The first two can be assessed through technical evaluation; the third can only be assessed through actual combat performance. BrahMos and Akashteer, after May 2025, have a credential that few competing systems globally can match — they have been used, in real combat, against a determined adversary, and they have performed as designed.

## Why the UAE is looking at Indian platforms

The UAE's interest in Indian defence platforms reflects a substantive strategic recalibration following the events of the 2026 Iran war. The UAE was directly targeted by Iranian drones and other weapon systems during the conflict, exposing vulnerabilities that the country's existing air-defence architecture — built around American THAAD missile-defence batteries, Patriot air-defence systems, and MGM-168 ATACMS missiles — could not fully address. The need to protect the Strait of Hormuz — through which a substantial portion of UAE energy exports transit — and the broader requirement to enhance the country's response capacity against regional threats have together driven a reassessment of UAE defence procurement.

The UAE's response has been to diversify its supplier base. Earlier in 2026, the country signed a memorandum of understanding with South Korea on defence cooperation reportedly worth more than USD 35 billion. The India discussions extend this diversification strategy — adding a third major source of advanced defence technology alongside the country's existing American suppliers and the new South Korean partnership. As one defence-cooperation analyst observed in the reporting, "a diversified supplier base gives the UAE more strategic autonomy, and closer ties with India have the added benefit of not antagonising the United States."

The Indian offering is particularly well-suited to the UAE's specific requirements. Akashteer's design philosophy — integrating multiple sensor platforms into a unified command architecture — complements rather than displaces the UAE's existing American systems. The UAE can deploy Akashteer to integrate THAAD, Patriot, and other systems into a more cohesive air-defence network, dramatically improving the operational coordination across what has historically been a fragmented multi-vendor defence architecture. BrahMos's offensive strike capability provides the UAE with a substantively new long-range precision strike capability that complements its existing arsenal.

## The Russia dimension

The BrahMos missile's joint development arrangement with Russia means that any export of the system requires Russian approval. This procedural requirement has historically been routine for established defence-export customers, and reporting from multiple sources suggests it is unlikely to become a substantive obstacle in the UAE case. Russia maintains strong bilateral ties with the UAE, and the broader Indo-Russian defence cooperation framework that has been the foundation of the BrahMos programme provides the institutional architecture within which export approvals are processed.

The pattern from the earlier BrahMos export to the Philippines — concluded in 2022 with a USD 375 million contract for three coastal-defence batteries — provides the template. The Philippines deal proceeded smoothly through the necessary approvals, and deliveries have progressed on schedule. The UAE discussion, if it advances to contract stage, would follow a similar institutional pathway.

## India-UAE: a multi-dimensional partnership

The defence discussions sit within a substantially broader India-UAE bilateral relationship that has, over the past decade, developed across virtually every dimension of contemporary international engagement. The Comprehensive Strategic Partnership established in 2017 anchored the framework. The Comprehensive Economic Partnership Agreement (CEPA) that came into force on 1 May 2022 — India's first major bilateral free-trade agreement with any country in over a decade — formalised the economic dimension. The Indian diaspora in the UAE — over 3.5 million people, the country's single largest expatriate community — provides the human foundation that anchors the relationship at the people-to-people level.

Bilateral trade between India and the UAE has, on the back of CEPA, expanded substantially — crossing USD 84 billion in 2023-24 and continuing to grow. The UAE is among India's top three trading partners and one of the largest sources of foreign direct investment into India. Indian companies have established substantial operations in the UAE; UAE companies have made substantial investments in Indian infrastructure, retail, real estate, financial services, and renewable energy. The currency-clearing arrangements between the two countries that began operationalising in 2023 have progressively reduced the dollar-intermediation requirement for bilateral trade.

The defence dimension has been building progressively. Defence exercises between the two militaries — naval exercises, air-force engagements, special-forces cooperation — have intensified over recent years. Defence-industrial cooperation has expanded. Joint training programmes have been initiated. The current BrahMos-Akashteer discussions extend this trajectory into the most consequential category of defence cooperation: the bilateral defence-equipment trade.

## India's defence-export trajectory

The UAE discussions arrive at a moment when India's defence-export trajectory has reached its most consequential phase. India's defence exports for the fiscal year ending March 2026 crossed USD 4 billion — equivalent to the ₹38,424 crore figure officially announced by the Ministry of Defence. This represents a more than 50-fold increase from the USD 7.26 million baseline of FY 2013-14. The exporter base has expanded to 145 active defence-exporting companies in FY 2025-26 — up 13.3 per cent year-on-year. Indian defence platforms now serve customers across more than 80 countries globally.

The specific portfolio of Indian defence exports has progressively expanded in both range and sophistication. BrahMos to the Philippines in 2022 was the inflection point — the first export of a complete advanced missile platform from India. Pinaka multi-barrel rocket launcher systems to Armenia in 2023 followed. Akash-1S surface-to-air missile systems to Armenia in 2024 added to the portfolio. The 155mm advanced towed artillery gun system, offshore patrol vessels, Dornier 228 aircraft, Chetak helicopters, fast interceptor boats, lightweight torpedoes — alongside an extensive component-and-subsystem ecosystem that serves customers including the United States, France, Russia, and multiple European countries — fill out the broader export footprint.

The UAE discussions, if they advance to contract stage, would add the Gulf region to India's substantive customer base in a category — air-defence command-and-control architecture — that has substantial follow-on potential. Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Oman, Bahrain, and Kuwait all face similar strategic-environment requirements and similar defence-architecture diversification needs. A successful UAE deployment would provide the operational reference point that subsequent Gulf-region defence procurement decisions would draw on.

## Other interested nations

The list of countries actively exploring acquisition of BrahMos has continued to expand. Beyond the Philippines (the established customer since 2022), reporting and statements over recent years have identified Armenia, Thailand, Singapore, Brunei, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Oman, Brazil, Chile, Argentina, Venezuela, and South Africa as countries actively considering acquisition. Some of these discussions are at very early stages; others are reportedly substantively further developed. The cumulative pipeline reflects the operational credibility BrahMos has acquired across multiple defence-procurement evaluations globally.

For Akashteer, the international interest is more recent — given that the system's operational validation occurred only with Operation Sindoor in May 2025 — but is reportedly growing rapidly. The Gulf region, with its complex multi-vendor air-defence architectures and the substantive command-and-control integration challenges that come with them, represents a particularly natural fit for Akashteer's design philosophy. Beyond the Gulf, several emerging-economy partners with substantial existing defence inventories from multiple sources are reportedly evaluating Akashteer for the systems-integration role.

## What it signifies strategically

The UAE-India defence discussions carry implications well beyond the immediate transaction. For India, the discussions validate the strategic logic that has underpinned more than a decade of investment in indigenous defence-manufacturing capability under the Aatmanirbhar Bharat doctrine. The Indian Government's progressive policy support — through the Defence Production and Export Promotion Policy 2020, the Defence Acquisition Procedure 2020, the Strategic Partnership model, the Production-Linked Incentive schemes, the positive indigenisation lists, the iDEX innovation framework, and the Defence Industrial Corridors — has built the institutional architecture within which BrahMos, Akashteer, and the broader ecosystem of Indian defence platforms have been developed. The international demand that these platforms are now attracting is the substantive return on that policy investment.

For the UAE, the Indian defence partnership extends the country's strategic-autonomy doctrine — diversifying defence procurement across multiple sources to reduce dependence on any single supplier. The combination of American, South Korean, and now Indian defence platforms provides the UAE with a substantively more resilient defence-acquisition architecture than reliance on any one supplier could deliver.

For the broader Indo-Gulf region, the discussions signal the emergence of a substantively new strategic geometry. India as a credible defence supplier to the Gulf — a region historically dependent on Western and increasingly Chinese suppliers — adds a new dimension to the regional security architecture. India's broader Indo-Pacific positioning, its role as a net security provider in the Indian Ocean Region, and its expanding strategic partnerships with multiple Gulf and West Asian countries collectively position the country as an increasingly consequential actor in West Asian security calculations.

For the post-Sindoor strategic environment, the UAE's interest in combat-validated Indian systems represents a substantive reputational dividend. The operational performance of BrahMos and Akashteer during Operation Sindoor has translated, within fourteen months, into substantive international demand. The strategic message — that India possesses, manufactures, and is willing to export defence capability that has been validated in actual combat conditions — has been received and acted upon by the most consequential category of potential customers: nations whose own security depends on the operational effectiveness of their defence platforms.

## The road ahead

The immediate next steps in the UAE-India discussions will involve detailed technical evaluation, commercial negotiation, and the procedural approvals required from both sides. For BrahMos specifically, Russian concurrence on the export will need to be formalised. For Akashteer, the customisation of the system for UAE-specific deployment requirements — integrating with the existing American defence architecture — will require substantive engineering work. Pricing, delivery schedules, training, maintenance, and lifecycle support all require detailed structuring.

In the medium term, the broader Indo-UAE defence cooperation will likely expand beyond the immediate platform sales. Joint defence-industrial ventures, technology-transfer arrangements, training-and-exercise cooperation, and the institutional architecture of sustained bilateral defence engagement will progressively develop. The UAE's substantial defence-industrial investment capacity, combined with India's manufacturing scale and growing engineering talent base, creates the foundation for ambitious bilateral defence-industrial cooperation that could extend well into the 2030s.

In the longer term, the precedent that the UAE-India defence relationship establishes will substantially shape the broader trajectory of India's defence-export footprint in the Gulf and beyond. The integration of Indian platforms into multi-vendor defence architectures of major customer nations creates the institutional pathway for sustained Indian defence-industry engagement at scale across multiple regions.

For now, on the morning of 25 June 2026, what stands on the record is this: the United Arab Emirates is in early-stage but rapidly-progressing talks with India for the potential acquisition of BrahMos supersonic cruise missiles and Akashteer air-defence command-and-control systems. The platforms in question were combat-validated in Operation Sindoor only fourteen months ago. India's defence exports for the just-concluded fiscal year crossed USD 4 billion. India's emergence as a credible global defence supplier, long aspired-to and progressively built, is now substantively underway. The Indo-Gulf strategic geometry has acquired a new dimension. And the Aatmanirbhar Bharat doctrine, applied to one of the most strategically consequential categories of industrial capability, is delivering — visibly, measurably, and on the global stage.
