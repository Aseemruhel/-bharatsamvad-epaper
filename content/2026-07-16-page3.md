LEAD: Ladakh's institutional architecture for democratic self-governance took a substantive leap forward on Monday, 13 July 2026, when Chief Secretary Ashish Kundra announced at a Leh press conference that the Union Territory Administration will constitute an Autonomous Hill Development Council in each of Ladakh's seven districts. The decision — described by the Chief Secretary as "a major step towards democratic decentralisation and grassroots governance" — extends elected local self-governance from the existing two Councils in Leh and Kargil to all seven districts of the Union Territory, following Ladakh's reorganisation from two districts to seven in April 2026 through the notification of Sham, Nubra, Changthang, Zanskar, and Drass as new administrative districts.

The announcement carries substantive institutional significance. Since Ladakh became a Union Territory in October 2019, elected representation had remained limited to the two Councils in Leh and Kargil, even as the region's administrative geography progressively expanded. Monday's announcement institutionally aligns the elected democratic structure with the expanded administrative structure, providing every district — including the five newly-notified districts — with the same tier of elected self-governance that has served Leh and Kargil substantively over the past three decades.

## The seven districts and the legal framework

The seven Autonomous Hill Development Councils will be constituted under Section 3(1) of the Ladakh Autonomous Hill Development Council (LAHDC) Act — the statute which already provides for the constitution of a Council in every district through a government notification published in the Official Gazette. The five new districts — Sham, Nubra, Changthang, Zanskar, and Drass — will therefore be brought within the existing legislative architecture that the Leh Council has operated under since 1995 and the Kargil Council since 2003.

Each of the seven Councils will exercise the full powers set out in the LAHDC Act. As the Chief Secretary emphasised in his press conference, the new districts will have "the same authority" as the existing Leh and Kargil Councils — "not a reduced version." The institutional equality of the seven Councils is a substantive design choice that reflects the Government's commitment to genuine democratic decentralisation rather than a tiered arrangement in which some districts hold greater authority than others.

## What the Councils will do

The powers vested in each District Council are substantive. **Land**: full authority over land ownership and land allotment within the district's territorial boundaries. **Employment**: control over recruitment and promotions for district cadre posts, meaning that employment decisions in each district will rest with an elected body within that district. **Finance**: authority to maintain a dedicated Council Fund and to levy taxes and fees, giving each district a revenue base of its own. **Development planning**: authority to prepare development plans and set local priorities, meaning each district will identify its own developmental needs rather than receive them from Leh or Kargil.

The functional portfolio of each Council extends across the sectors that define daily life in Ladakh's high-altitude districts: health, education, tourism, local infrastructure, and social welfare schemes. The empowerment is comprehensive — each Council will substantively govern the local dimensions of these consequential sectors within its territorial jurisdiction.

## The Article 371 apex body

The Chief Secretary also outlined the administration's proposal for a Union Territory-level institution above the seven Councils under a customised Article 371 framework. Article 371 of the Constitution provides for special provisions applicable to certain states — including provisions that have historically supported the unique governance requirements of Nagaland, Sikkim, Mizoram, Arunachal Pradesh, and other states with distinctive geographic, demographic, and cultural characteristics.

The proposed Ladakh apex body would exercise legislative, executive, financial, and administrative powers — a substantially comprehensive institutional mandate that would provide the Union Territory with its first genuinely elected legislative institution. The Chief Secretary described the proposed framework as "a first-of-its-kind model drawing on the best features of similar constitutional and administrative arrangements elsewhere in the country" — a substantive characterisation that reflects both the ambition and the institutional novelty of the arrangement.

The structure and powers of the Union Territory-level body will be finalised through consultation between Ladakh's representatives and the Government of India. Consultations with the Ministry of Home Affairs are already underway. Some rebalancing of powers between the district Councils and the UT-level body may follow — a natural outcome of finalising the specific institutional architecture within which the two tiers will operate.

## The three-tier elected structure

The combined effect of the announcement is to establish a genuine three-tier elected democratic structure across Ladakh — a first for the Union Territory. Panchayati Raj institutions will continue to function at the village level, providing elected representation at the most immediate level of local governance. The seven Autonomous Hill Development Councils will provide elected representation at the district level. And the proposed Article 371 apex body will provide elected representation at the Union Territory level.

The architecture responds substantively to long-standing demands for greater decentralisation that have been articulated by Ladakhi civil society, elected representatives from Leh and Kargil, and community organisations across the region. The demand for meaningful autonomous governance — anchored in the unique geographical, cultural, ecological, and strategic characteristics of Ladakh — has been consistent since well before the region's transition to Union Territory status in 2019, and Monday's announcement provides the substantive institutional response.

## What comes next

Before elections to the new Councils can be held, minor amendments to the LAHDC Act are required alongside the delimitation of constituencies for the five new districts. The administration is expected to initiate the delimitation and related formalities in the coming weeks. No specific timeline for the elections was announced during Monday's press conference; officials indicated that the process would move forward at the pace required to complete the necessary institutional preparations.

Alongside the Councils' constitution, the administration has indicated that additional district-level posts will be created in phases as the administrative structure expands, senior officers will undertake regular visits to the newly created districts — especially remote and border areas — for grievance redressal and monitoring of development projects, and consultations on the Article 371 apex-body framework will progress in parallel.

## What it signifies

For Ladakh, the announcement marks the substantive translation of Union Territory status into meaningful democratic self-governance across every district. For India's broader federal architecture, the emerging Ladakh model — three tiers of elected representation combining Panchayats, Hill Councils, and an Article 371 apex body — provides a substantively new institutional design that may serve as a reference model for governance in other unique geographical and cultural contexts. For the strategically consequential Himalayan Union Territory, the deepening of democratic self-governance strengthens the substantive integration of local self-determination with national strategic interest.

For now, on the morning of 14 July 2026, what stands on the institutional record is this: seven Autonomous Hill Development Councils where there were two; a proposed Union Territory-level apex body under a customised Article 371 framework; a three-tier elected democratic structure spanning village, district, and Union Territory; and the substantive fulfilment of long-standing demands for genuine democratic decentralisation across Ladakh. The Chief Secretary described it as "the first firm step" toward a new governance model. On the substantive evidence of Monday's announcement, that description appears entirely justified.
