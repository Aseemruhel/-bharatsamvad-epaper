LEAD: Russian President Vladimir Putin used his annual briefing with heads of international news agencies, on the sidelines of the 29th St. Petersburg International Economic Forum, to deliver one of the most expansive recent public assessments by a Russian head of state of India's economic trajectory and its place in the global order. India, he said, was now one of the world's leading economies with some of the highest growth rates globally — and these achievements, he stressed, were the product of sustained effort rather than chance.

In remarks credited specifically to the work done under the leadership of Prime Minister Narendra Modi, the Russian President framed the bilateral relationship as a "special privileged strategic partnership" built up over decades, and was unequivocal that India's expanding engagements with the West — including the United States — pose no obstacle, in his view, to the depth of the India-Russia partnership.

## The setting

The remarks were delivered at the traditional briefing for heads of international news agencies, organised by Russia's TASS news agency on the sidelines of the SPIEF, which is being held in St. Petersburg from 3 to 6 June 2026 under the theme "Pragmatic Dialogue: the Path to a Stable Future". The annual forum is being attended this year by around 20,000 participants from approximately 100 countries — including, for the first time in a decade, an official United States delegation, alongside large delegations from India, China, Saudi Arabia (this year's guest country), ASEAN, and several African and Latin American partners.

The news-agency briefing is widely regarded as the most candid setting of the SPIEF, since it allows for an extended question-and-answer format on bilateral and global issues. Putin's remarks on India came in response to questions on the bilateral relationship, the impact of evolving US-India ties on Moscow's calculations, and India's broader economic trajectory.

## On India's economic ascent

The Russian President's framing on India's economy was unusually direct. India, he said, has made "great and important achievements in its development" — achievements he tied to the work and talent of the Indian people, and to the leadership of Prime Minister Modi. He underlined that progress at this scale does not happen by accident, but is the result of consistent, sustained effort.

In tone and substance, Putin's remarks aligned with the broader Russian read of India: as a partner whose strategic weight is increasing in ways that make the relationship valuable on its own terms, separate from any tactical considerations arising from Moscow's other foreign-policy positions.

## The historical depth Putin invoked

A striking feature of the remarks was the explicit historical framing. Putin anchored the relationship not to recent years but to 1947 — the year of India's independence and the establishment of diplomatic ties between the Soviet Union and the Indian Republic. He framed the early Soviet support for an independent India as a foundational decision that has carried forward in unbroken form through the post-Cold War transition and into the present.

That framing is consistent with how the relationship has historically been read in Indian foreign-policy circles: as a strategic continuity that has survived the dissolution of the USSR, the post-Cold War realignment, India's economic opening, and the more recent re-positioning of Russia under sanctions. India is one of the few major economies that has maintained substantive engagement with Russia through the post-2022 phase, including significant purchases of Russian crude oil and continued movement on long-running defence procurements.

## The US-India question

Asked specifically about whether India's evolving relationship with the United States affects India-Russia ties, Putin's response was notable for what it did not contain: no warning, no implicit pressure, no suggestion that the relationship was conditional. India, he said, is a country of 1.5 billion people, a large economy, the largest democracy — and it is natural that it should develop economic relations with those countries it deems necessary.

The framing — that India is entitled to pursue its interests across the international system — is precisely the framing that Indian foreign-policy doctrine has long sought to assert. New Delhi has consistently positioned itself as a country that engages with the United States, Russia, China, the European Union, the Quad, BRICS, the SCO, and West Asian partners simultaneously, with each track managed on its own merits. The fact that the Russian president publicly endorses that strategic autonomy is itself a useful diplomatic data point for India.

## The defence dimension

Putin's praise was accompanied, in the same news-agency briefing, by a fresh sales pitch: the Russian president presented the Sukhoi Su-57 — Russia's fifth-generation stealth fighter — as the world's best combat aircraft, framing it as a possible next step in India-Russia defence cooperation. The pitch comes in the same week that India received the fourth of five contracted S-400 Sudarshan squadrons from Russia, a long-delayed delivery the completion of which has been a significant data point of its own.

For India, the Su-57 question is a complex one. New Delhi is simultaneously procuring the Rafale from France and pursuing the indigenous Advanced Medium Combat Aircraft programme; any decision on a Russian fifth-generation platform would need to be weighed against those existing tracks, against questions of technology transfer, and against the implications for India's relationships with Western suppliers. The pitch is real; the response, if any, will be measured and slow.

## The longer framework

In the background of Putin's remarks sits a more structured initiative: the Russia-India long-term cooperation plan, targeted to run till 2030. Putin had announced at SPIEF 2025 that this framework was being advanced as part of Moscow's broader approach to its key strategic partners. The plan covers economic, energy, defence and connectivity cooperation, and is being finalised.

The 2030 horizon is a meaningful signal. It implies that both governments view the partnership as something to be planned and structured across an extended timeline, rather than something managed reactively in response to short-term events.

## Why this matters

Putin's choice to use the SPIEF 2026 stage to publicly elevate India is not incidental. With Russia continuing to operate under Western sanctions and seeking to demonstrate the resilience of its international engagements, the visibility of India in his remarks signals both the value Moscow places on the relationship and the continuity it expects to maintain.

For India, the public endorsement from the head of one of its longest-standing strategic partners has tangible value — particularly as New Delhi navigates a complex post-Operation Sindoor regional environment, an evolving Indo-Pacific architecture, and the broader churn in the global economy. India's diplomatic doctrine of strategic autonomy is built on the premise that the country can maintain substantive ties across blocs without being claimed by any one of them. Putin's remarks, in their substance and tone, accept that doctrine on its own terms.

## The road ahead

The next visible milestones in the relationship will be the finalisation of the 2030 cooperation plan, the possible upcoming Russia visit by PM Modi (the timing of which has been the subject of routine Indian press speculation), and any follow-through on the Su-57 pitch — which will, realistically, take years rather than months. In the immediate term, the SPIEF 2026 remarks are a public signpost. The deeper test, as always, will be what is delivered through working-level engagements between the two governments over the months that follow.
