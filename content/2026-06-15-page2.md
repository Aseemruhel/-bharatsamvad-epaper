LEAD: India's merchandise exports hit a new monthly record of US$ 45.20 billion in May 2026, with total exports — merchandise and services combined — reaching US$ 81.96 billion, the Ministry of Commerce and Industry's quick estimates released on Monday, 15 June 2026, have confirmed. The figure represents a positive growth of 15.83 per cent over total exports of US$ 70.76 billion in May 2025, and reflects what the Ministry has described as robust external demand for Indian goods and services. The monthly merchandise export figure has been specifically highlighted by the Commerce Ministry as a "new record high".

The cumulative figures for the first two months of fiscal year 2026-27 — April and May — extend the positive picture. Total exports for April-May 2026-27 are estimated at US$ 162.69 billion, compared to US$ 141.89 billion during the corresponding period of 2025-26, an increase of 14.66 per cent. Within this, merchandise exports for the two-month period totalled US$ 88.91 billion against US$ 76.59 billion a year earlier — a positive growth of 16.09 per cent.

## The drivers

Five sectors emerged as the principal drivers of the May 2026 merchandise export performance. Petroleum products led, with exports rising 54.89 per cent to US$ 8.42 billion from US$ 5.44 billion in May 2025 — reflecting the strength of India's refining capacity and its position as one of the world's significant exporters of refined petroleum products. Engineering goods, India's largest single export category by value, rose 24.48 per cent to US$ 12.31 billion from US$ 9.89 billion a year earlier. Organic and inorganic chemicals exports increased 12.71 per cent to US$ 2.72 billion. Electronic goods, reflecting the steady expansion of India's electronics manufacturing base, rose 11.62 per cent to US$ 5.10 billion. Gems and jewellery exports increased 6.66 per cent to US$ 2.53 billion.

Beyond these headline sectors, a wider range of categories registered positive growth. Other cereals exports rose by a striking 262.73 per cent. Meat, dairy and poultry products exports grew 42.51 per cent. Oil meals rose 24.61 per cent. Handicrafts excluding handmade carpets grew 23.85 per cent. Plastics and linoleum exports rose 21.82 per cent. Iron ore exports grew 14.76 per cent. Drugs and pharmaceuticals — India's flagship export category for healthcare manufacturing — grew 6.13 per cent. Rice exports rose 5.38 per cent. Cereal preparations and processed items grew 4.4 per cent. Coffee exports grew 4.37 per cent.

The breadth of the positive growth across sectors — covering energy, industrial, electronics, agricultural, pharmaceutical, and processed-food categories — indicates that the May export performance was not concentrated in any single category but reflects broad-based strength across the Indian export portfolio.

## The services strength

Beyond the headline merchandise numbers, the services trade picture remained one of structural strength for the Indian external sector. Services exports for May 2026 were estimated at US$ 36.76 billion, up from US$ 32.46 billion in May 2025 — an increase of approximately 13.2 per cent year-on-year. For the cumulative April-May 2026-27 period, services exports reached US$ 73.79 billion against US$ 65.30 billion in April-May 2025-26.

The services sector continues to deliver a meaningful trade surplus for India. The services trade surplus for April-May 2026-27 is estimated at US$ 36.31 billion, compared to US$ 31.69 billion in the corresponding period of the previous year — a year-on-year expansion of approximately 14.6 per cent. Services exports overall are estimated to grow 12.99 per cent during April-May 2026-27 over the corresponding period of 2025-26.

The strength of services exports reflects the continued global competitiveness of India's information technology services, business process management, professional consulting, financial services, and emerging higher-value categories including research and development services and global capability centres. The structural expansion of the services trade surplus — visible across the cumulative two-month figure — reinforces the positioning of services as one of the most consistent contributors to India's external sector performance.

## The non-petroleum performance

A further indicator of the broad-based strength of the export performance is the non-petroleum exports figure — a metric watched by trade economists because it strips out the volatility of petroleum-product exports, which can be influenced as much by global crude prices as by underlying volume changes. The cumulative non-petroleum exports for April-May 2026-27 were valued at US$ 70.74 billion, an increase of 10.49 per cent compared to US$ 64.03 billion in April-May 2025-26.

The non-petroleum and non-gems & jewellery export figure, which strips out two of the most price-sensitive categories, was US$ 65.89 billion for April-May 2026-27 compared to US$ 59.15 billion in the same period of the previous year — a steady increase that reflects underlying volume growth across the core manufacturing and services export base.

## The geographic shifts

The Commerce Ministry's data also points to a meaningful diversification in India's export geography. The top five destinations exhibiting the highest year-on-year growth in May 2026 were Singapore (with exports rising 68.96 per cent), South Africa (116.21 per cent), Tanzania (196.89 per cent), Italy (87.54 per cent), and Sri Lanka (150.29 per cent). For the cumulative April-May 2026-27 period, the top five fastest-growing destinations were Singapore (123.75 per cent), Tanzania (172.22 per cent), Sri Lanka (183.32 per cent), South Africa (62.24 per cent), and China (25.85 per cent).

This pattern reflects the steady deepening of India's economic engagement with both its immediate South Asian neighbourhood — through the substantial growth in exports to Sri Lanka — and with Africa, where exports to Tanzania and South Africa have grown at substantial rates. The growth in exports to Italy and to Singapore reinforces the strength of India's connections to advanced economies and to major Asian trading hubs respectively.

## What it confirms

The May 2026 trade data, read alongside the cumulative April-May figures, confirms several positive elements of India's external sector picture. The record monthly merchandise export figure of US$ 45.20 billion sets a new high for the country's outbound shipments, exceeding all previous monthly records. The 15.83 per cent year-on-year growth in total exports is the strongest such figure in several months. The breadth of sectoral participation in the export growth — spanning petroleum, engineering, electronics, chemicals, jewellery, pharmaceuticals, and agricultural products — indicates that the performance is not narrowly concentrated.

The cumulative April-May figures, by extending the strong May performance over a two-month base, suggest that the trajectory of the new fiscal year has begun on a robust footing. The cumulative growth of 14.66 per cent in total exports for the first two months of fiscal year 2026-27 compares favourably to most recent corresponding two-month periods.

## The wider context

The trade performance lands in a wider economic environment in which India's growth narrative remains substantively strong. The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development, in its most recent interim Economic Outlook, has projected India's GDP growth at 7.6 per cent for fiscal year 2025-26 — placing India as the world's fastest-growing major economy by a comfortable margin. Strong export performance is one of the indicators consistent with the broader growth picture that the OECD and other multilateral bodies have identified.

The Commerce Ministry has, in its accompanying statements, characterised the May figures as evidence of "robust external demand" for Indian goods and services. The expansion of merchandise exports to a new monthly record, against the backdrop of global trade conditions that have included supply-chain disruptions, tariff tensions and Middle East energy market volatility, is being read as a measure of the underlying competitiveness and resilience of the Indian export sector.

## The road ahead

For the remaining months of fiscal year 2026-27, the trade data will be watched for whether the strong April-May trajectory is sustained. The Commerce Ministry has set out the policy objective of further expanding India's global trade footprint, with particular emphasis on growing the country's share in higher-value manufacturing exports, advanced services exports, and emerging sector categories such as electronics, semiconductors, renewable energy equipment and mobility products.

The continued expansion of services exports — already a structural strength — combined with the broadening manufacturing export base, is expected to drive the medium-term trajectory. The diversification of export destinations across South Asian, African, European and East Asian markets, visible in the May data, contributes to the resilience of the overall picture.

For now, what the May 2026 data establishes is a strong, record-setting performance: India's merchandise exports at their highest-ever monthly level, total exports growing at double-digit rates, services exports continuing their structural expansion, and the diversification of the country's export geography proceeding apace.
