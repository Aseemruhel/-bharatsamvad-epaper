LEAD: Detection of counterfeit currency in India’s banking system rose by 5.7% in financial year 2025–26, according to the latest annual report total number of fake notes identified increased to 2,29,746 pieces, up from 2,17,396 in the previous year, with the sharpest pressure seen in high-circulation denominations such as ₹500 and ₹200.

Officials noted that while the overall increase is moderate in percentage terms, the shift in denomination-wise distribution highlights evolving patterns in counterfeiting activity within India’s cash economy.

## What RBI data shows

The central bank’s annual report indicates that ₹500 notes remained the most frequently counterfeited denomination, rising 20.5% to 1,41,907 pieces in FY26. This continues to reflect the dominance of ₹500 notes in daily cash transactions following demonetisation and subsequent currency redesign.

Counterfeit ₹200 notes, however, declined to 30,591 pieces from 32,660 a year earlier, suggesting improved detection or reduced circulation of fakes in that segment.

At the lower end of the spectrum, ₹20 notes saw one of the sharpest surges, jumping 47.4% to 373 pieces from 253 in FY25. Analysts often view such spikes in small denominations as indicators of wider distribution attempts, as smaller-value counterfeit notes are easier to circulate in informal transactions.

By contrast, counterfeit ₹100 notes fell to 45,621 from 51,069, and ₹50 notes also declined to 10,274 from 12,015. The sharpest structural drop continued in ₹2,000 notes, which fell to 824 pieces from 3,508, consistent with their phased withdrawal from circulation.

## At a Glance

- Total counterfeit notes detected: **2,29,746 (FY26)** vs 2,17,396 (FY25)  
- Overall increase: **+5.7% year-on-year**  
- ₹500 notes: **1,41,907 (+20.5%) — highest share of detections**  
- ₹200 notes: **30,591 (down from 32,660)**  
- ₹100 notes: **45,621 (down from 51,069)**  
- ₹50 notes: **10,274 (down from 12,015)**  
- ₹20 notes: **373 (up 47.4%) — sharpest percentage rise**  
- ₹2,000 notes: **824 (down sharply from 3,508)**  
- Majority detection: **97.6% at commercial banks; 2.4% by RBI directly**

## Denomination-wise pattern shift

The data suggests a clear concentration of counterfeiting activity in widely used high-value notes, particularly ₹500 currency, which dominates cash transactions in retail and wholesale markets.

Meanwhile, declines in ₹100 and ₹50 segments may indicate either improved security feature effectiveness or reduced attractiveness of lower-value counterfeiting due to higher detection risks relative to payoff.

The continued fall in ₹2,000 counterfeit detection aligns with its reduced presence in circulation following its phased withdrawal from active use.

## What RBI said in the report

The RBI clarified that counterfeit detection data reflects notes identified within the banking system and does not include seizures by law enforcement agencies.

It also noted that banks continue to remain the primary detection point, accounting for nearly all identified fake currency in circulation channels.

## Why this matters

Even modest increases in counterfeit detection can have broader implications for cash-dependent segments of the economy. Fake currency undermines transaction trust, increases verification costs for banks and retailers, and places additional burden on cash-handling infrastructure.

The concentration in ₹500 notes is particularly significant because they function as the backbone of India’s physical currency system. Any sustained rise in counterfeiting in this denomination can force tighter authentication protocols, slower cash handling, and increased reliance on electronic verification systems.

At the same time, the data reinforces the ongoing transition in India toward digital payments, where counterfeit risk is structurally lower.

## Structural context

India’s currency system has undergone significant redesign and security upgrades over the past decade, particularly after demonetisation and the introduction of new Mahatma Gandhi series notes.

Despite these measures, counterfeit activity persists, often adapting to newer security features. Authorities typically respond through improved ink technologies, security threads, and enhanced banknote design cycles.

The relatively low detection numbers in smaller denominations suggest that counterfeiters are focusing resources on notes with higher transactional value per unit.

## Outlook

Going forward, trends in counterfeit detection will likely depend on three factors: updates to currency security features, enforcement efficiency at bank-level verification systems, and the broader shift toward digital transactions.

The next annual cycle will be closely watched to see whether the rise in FY26 represents a short-term fluctuation or the beginning of a sustained upward trend in counterfeit pressure.
