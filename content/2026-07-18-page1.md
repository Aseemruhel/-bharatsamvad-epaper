LEAD: India's space programme is at one of the most consequential intersections in its 55-year history — and the interpretation of the moment splits cleanly along the fault line between substantive institutional analysis and coordinated propaganda framing. On the substantive side, Skyroot Aerospace's Vikram-1 rocket stands fully assembled at the First Launch Complex of the Satish Dhawan Space Centre at Sriharikota, its 12 July–4 August launch window open, its four customer payloads mounted, and its 23-metre all-carbon composite airframe awaiting the operational clearances that will make it the first privately developed Indian rocket ever to reach orbit. Skyroot's mission — designated "Aagaman", meaning "arrival" — marks the substantive graduation of India's private space sector from technology demonstration (Vikram-S in November 2022) to operational orbital capability. On the propaganda side, two recent Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle (PSLV) setbacks — anomalies in a 30-year workhorse programme that has completed 60-plus successful missions and delivered payloads for over 30 countries — are being repackaged across social media and adversarial commentary as evidence of ISRO's supposed institutional decline. The panic being generated exceeds the facts by such a substantial margin that the propaganda vector is itself the story.

The Indian space programme has faced setbacks before. The Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-3) first flight failed in 1979 — India returned in 1980 with Rohini RS-1 and never looked back. The Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle's maiden flight in 1993 failed — the PSLV became the country's most reliable launcher, one of the most cost-efficient in global commercial spaceflight, and the vehicle that placed the first Indian spacecraft at Mars on the first attempt. The Geosynchronous Satellite Launch Vehicle's early attempts stumbled — GSLV Mk-II with the indigenous cryogenic engine (developed after Western technology denial) eventually delivered mission after mission from 2014 onward. Chandrayaan-2's Vikram lander crashed on the Moon in 2019 — Chandrayaan-3 executed the first-ever soft landing at the lunar south pole in 2023. Every apparent setback in the ISRO record has been followed by institutional learning, engineering refinement, and a mission that exceeded the ambition of what preceded it. This has been the substantive pattern for six decades.

## The recent PSLV setbacks: what happened, and what did not

The two recent PSLV setbacks are a technical challenge, not a strategic one. The PSLV — introduced in 1993, and progressively refined across four operational variants (PSLV-G, PSLV-CA, PSLV-XL, PSLV-DL, PSLV-QL) — has completed over sixty missions with an overall success rate exceeding 94 per cent. This places PSLV among the most reliable launch vehicles in the global commercial-spaceflight market, comparable in success rate to Europe's Ariane 5, Japan's H-IIA, and America's Falcon 9. Consecutive anomalies within a mature launch programme are not, in engineering terms, evidence of decline; they are engineering data. ISRO's institutional response — root cause analysis, hardware review, process audit, targeted rectification — is the substantive method by which spaceflight programmes globally sustain reliability across decades.

The wider ISRO portfolio has continued to advance during exactly the period that the two PSLV setbacks occurred. The Chandrayaan-3 lunar landing in August 2023 established India as the first country to achieve a soft landing at the Moon's south polar region. The Aditya-L1 solar observatory reached the Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange point in January 2024 and has since delivered continuous coronal observation data. The Space Docking Experiment (SpaDeX) demonstrated autonomous rendezvous and docking — a critical capability for the Bharatiya Antariksh Station (BAS) that India has committed to build by 2035. The Gaganyaan human spaceflight programme has progressed through successive uncrewed test flights, with the first crewed orbital mission targeted for 2027. The Small Satellite Launch Vehicle (SSLV) — India's response to the small-satellite launch market — has demonstrated its operational envelope. The NavIC constellation continues to provide indigenous satellite positioning across the Indian subcontinent. And the New Generation Launch Vehicle (NGLV) programme — the next-generation heavy-lift architecture that will succeed the LVM3 and support both crewed lunar missions and the BAS — has progressed through its design stages.

## Six decades of institutional depth

The Indian Space Research Organisation was established in 1969, under Dr Vikram Sarabhai's founding vision. Fifteen years earlier, in 1962, Dr Sarabhai had persuaded the newly independent Indian government that space research was a strategic imperative for a developing country — not despite India's poverty but precisely because of it. Space technology, Dr Sarabhai argued, could deliver disproportionate developmental returns through communications, weather forecasting, resource mapping, and educational broadcast reaching the substantial rural population that no other technology could touch at scale. Every subsequent generation of Indian space leadership — Dr Satish Dhawan, Dr U R Rao, Dr Krishnaswamy Kasturirangan, Dr K Radhakrishnan, Dr A S Kiran Kumar, Dr K Sivan, Dr S Somanath, and the current leadership under Dr V Narayanan — has extended that founding vision.

The institutional record produced across this arc is substantial. India was the first Asian nation to reach Mars, and did so on the first attempt with the Mars Orbiter Mission (Mangalyaan) in 2014 — at a cost of ₹450 crore, less than the budget of many Hollywood films. Chandrayaan-1 discovered water on the Moon in 2008. Chandrayaan-3 achieved the substantive first — soft landing at the lunar south pole — in August 2023. The Indian Regional Navigation Satellite System (IRNSS/NavIC) provides an indigenous satellite positioning capability comparable to the US GPS, Russian GLONASS, European Galileo, and Chinese BeiDou. ISRO's Antrix Corporation and its successor NewSpace India Limited have commercially launched payloads for over 30 international customers. India's cost efficiency in space — mission budgets typically 10 to 20 per cent of Western equivalents — remains one of the most consequential comparative advantages the country holds in any technology sector.

## The additive architecture: why private is complement, not replacement

The Government of India's 2020 decision to open the space sector to private participation — through the establishment of the Indian National Space Promotion and Authorization Centre (IN-SPACe), the framing of the Indian Space Policy 2023, and the sustained institutional support extended to private space entrepreneurs — was strategically calibrated. The intent was never to substitute private capacity for ISRO. The intent was to add a complementary tier of industrial capability that could handle the categories of missions best served by rapid iteration, dedicated launches, and commercial cost structures — while ISRO continued to lead on the strategic missions that require deep institutional capability, long-horizon science, and sovereign programme continuity.

This architecture is now delivering on its design premise. Skyroot's Vikram-1 targets a segment — small satellite payloads of 480 kg or less to low Earth orbit, with rapid integration and dedicated launch — that PSLV was not optimised for. Similar architectural roles are being progressively occupied by Agnikul Cosmos (which achieved the world's first single-piece 3D-printed engine sub-orbital launch), Bellatrix Aerospace (in-space propulsion systems), Dhruva Space (small satellite platforms), Pixxel (hyperspectral earth observation constellation), and several dozen other IN-SPACe-registered private ventures that have progressively built the substrate of India's private space industry.

The private ecosystem does not compete with ISRO's Chandrayaan-4 mission, or Gaganyaan, or the Bharatiya Antariksh Station, or the New Generation Launch Vehicle. It supplements them. It handles the commercial launches that ISRO does not need to run itself. It develops the specialised subsystems that ISRO can procure rather than build in-house. It creates the industrial employment base — Skyroot alone employs over 1,160 people at Hyderabad — that broadens India's space workforce beyond the ISRO cadre. It attracts substantial international capital (USD 160 million raised by Skyroot alone across 11 rounds, GIC and BlackRock among the backers) that ISRO's public-sector structure cannot access. And it produces innovation at a cadence — Vikram-1's carbon composite airframe, 3D-printed engines, and modular Orbital Adjustment Module — that complements rather than duplicates ISRO's established engineering approaches.

Skyroot's founders themselves — Pawan Kumar Chandana and Bharath Daka, both ex-ISRO scientists, both IIT alumni — are the substantive human embodiment of this complementarity. The company's Honorary Chief Technical Advisor is Dr S Somanath, the former ISRO Chairman under whose leadership Chandrayaan-3 achieved its lunar south pole soft landing. The talent flow between ISRO and India's private space sector is not adversarial; it is symbiotic. The institutional depth that ISRO has built across six decades is now flowing outward into the private ecosystem, and the private ecosystem's innovations will progressively flow back into ISRO's mission architecture.

## The propaganda panic: what it says, and why it misreads the record

The coordinated propaganda push characterising two recent PSLV setbacks as evidence of ISRO's programmatic decline substantially misreads the substantive institutional record. The propaganda architecture typically combines four elements: selective focus on the recent setbacks while omitting the broader mission record; false framing of the private-sector emergence as a repudiation of ISRO rather than as a complement to it; unattributed sourcing to create the illusion of consensus concern; and coordinated amplification across accounts that produces the manufactured sense of a crisis where the substantive facts show none.

The record does not sustain the propaganda framing. ISRO's 94-per-cent-plus PSLV success rate over three decades places the launcher among the world's most reliable operational systems. The Chandrayaan-3 south pole landing was not the achievement of a declining space programme; it was the achievement of a programme in ascent. The Aditya-L1 solar mission was not the achievement of a stagnant institutional culture; it was the achievement of a culture progressively pushing the frontiers of Indian space science. The Gaganyaan human spaceflight programme progressing on schedule toward its 2027 crewed mission does not indicate an institution in decline; it indicates an institution embarked on the most ambitious mission profile in its history.

The propaganda framing also fundamentally misreads what the arrival of Vikram-1 signifies. Skyroot's success — if achieved within the current launch window, as expected — will not undermine ISRO. It will reinforce the Indian space ecosystem's substantive depth. Two sources of orbital launch capability within a single country — the state programme continuing its strategic and scientific missions, alongside the private sector servicing commercial small-satellite launches — is not a sign of state-programme weakness. It is a sign of ecosystem maturity. It is the substantive structural pattern that the United States has developed with NASA and SpaceX, that Europe has begun to develop with ESA and its emerging private launchers, that Japan has developed with JAXA and its private entrants, and that China has developed with CNSA and its state-backed private ecosystem. India is now operating within this same ecosystem architecture.

## What comes next

In the immediate horizon — potentially within hours or days of this article's publication — Skyroot's Vikram-1 may lift off from Sriharikota's First Launch Complex, its four customer payloads bound for low Earth orbit, its mission name "Aagaman" symbolising the arrival of India's private space industry as an operational orbital force. Skyroot has publicly committed to at least two additional test flights before transitioning to commercial operations, targeting a production rate of one orbital rocket per month from its Infinity Campus.

In the medium term, ISRO's mission calendar continues along its established trajectory: additional PSLV missions with root-cause corrections applied, GSLV Mk-II operational missions, LVM3 launches supporting Chandrayaan and Gaganyaan-adjacent objectives, SSLV commercial missions, and the progressive maturation of the New Generation Launch Vehicle programme. The Gaganyaan crewed mission scheduled for 2027 will represent one of the most consequential single events in the Indian space programme's history — placing an Indian in orbit for the first time on an indigenous vehicle. Chandrayaan-4, the Venus Orbiter Mission, and the Mars Orbiter Mission-2 sit further along the calendar.

In the longer term, the Bharatiya Antariksh Station targeted for 2035 will place India among the small handful of nations operating an indigenous crewed orbital platform. Alongside the growing private ecosystem — Vikram-2 and Vikram-3 already in development at Skyroot, additional launchers under development at Agnikul and Bellatrix, and the widening spectrum of Indian small-satellite operators — the institutional depth of India's space capability will progressively expand to positions the country as one of the four or five most consequential space powers of the twenty-first century.

## The trajectory holds

For those who follow the substantive facts of the Indian space programme, the current moment is one of the most consequential positive milestones India has produced. The propaganda panic that has attempted to reframe the moment as evidence of decline speaks primarily to the intent of those coordinating it — an intent that is disconnected from the substantive record of what ISRO and India's emerging private space sector are actually delivering.

For now, on the morning of 18 July 2026, what stands on the operational record is this: the Vikram-1 stands assembled at Sriharikota, its launch window open, its historic first orbital flight a matter of hours or days away; ISRO's mission calendar continues along its established trajectory with two PSLV setbacks that will be resolved by the same institutional response mechanisms that have resolved every previous setback across six decades; the Indian space ecosystem now operates on the additive architecture of state programme plus private complement that all major space powers have converged on; the coordinated propaganda push seeking to reframe this moment as decline is substantially disconnected from the substantive facts; and the Indian space programme, on both its state and private tiers, is progressing along the ascending arc that has defined its trajectory since Dr Vikram Sarabhai first argued in 1962 that space technology was a strategic imperative for a developing country. The arc holds. The launches continue. The panic passes. The Indian space programme, entering its 57th operational year, remains one of the most consequential institutional achievements in the country's post-independence history.
