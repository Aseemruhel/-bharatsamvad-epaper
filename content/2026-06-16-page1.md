LEAD: The Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) on Monday, 15 June 2026, successfully conducted a flight test of the Long Range Land Attack Cruise Missile (LRLACM) — India's indigenously developed long-range subsonic cruise missile — from Dr APJ Abdul Kalam Island, off the coast of Odisha. According to data captured by tracking instruments deployed at the Integrated Test Range (ITR) at Chandipur, all mission objectives were fully met. Defence Minister Rajnath Singh congratulated the DRDO team and industry partners; Defence Secretary and Chairman, DRDO, Rajesh Kumar Singh, who monitored the launch activities directly, congratulated team members involved in the test.

The launch was witnessed by senior officials from DRDO and user representatives from the Indian Navy and the Indian Air Force — the two services that, along with the Indian Army, are expected to be the principal operators of the LRLACM once it is formally inducted into service.

## What the LRLACM is

The Long Range Land Attack Cruise Missile is an indigenous subsonic cruise missile designed to deliver precision strikes against strategic land-based targets over extended distances. Designed to operate at very low altitudes — flying in terrain-hugging mode and manoeuvring around obstacles — it presents a fundamentally different signature profile to defending radars compared to ballistic missiles, which follow predictable arching trajectories that modern air defence systems are increasingly capable of intercepting. A subsonic cruise missile that hugs terrain remains below the radar horizon for much of its flight, giving target air-defence systems a substantially compressed reaction window.

The technical envelope of the LRLACM, on the basis of available defence-press reporting, includes a range of up to approximately 1,500 kilometres, a cruise speed in the subsonic regime (Mach 0.7-0.9), and a cruise altitude that ranges between approximately 50 metres at low-level terrain-following and around 4 kilometres for higher-altitude transit phases. The missile is approximately six metres in length, around half a metre in diameter, weighs in the order of one tonne, and has a wingspan of approximately 2.7 metres with fold-out wings configured for vertical-launch deployment. It is designed to carry both conventional and, by class, nuclear-capable warheads.

Critically, the LRLACM is configured for multi-platform deployment. The land-attack variant can be fired from a mobile articulated launcher of the kind used in the maiden flight test from ITR Chandipur in November 2024. The sea variant is designed for compatibility with the Universal Vertical Launcher Module (UVLM) that is already deployed across the frontline ships of the Indian Navy for the BrahMos cruise missile — meaning Indian warships can carry both supersonic BrahMos and subsonic LRLACM rounds without a structural reconfiguration. An air-launched variant is being developed for carriage and release from the Su-30 MKI fighter aircraft, using the same underwing pylon arrangement that already carries BrahMos-A. A submarine-launched variant is also planned.

## The Aatmanirbharta dimension

The most strategically significant feature of the LRLACM, beyond its operational specifications, is the depth of its indigenous content. The Aeronautical Development Establishment (ADE) in Bengaluru serves as the nodal laboratory for the programme, coordinating the design and integration. All subsystems have been developed by various DRDO laboratories in partnership with Indian industry. Bharat Dynamics Limited (BDL), the public-sector defence manufacturing company, is the principal industrial partner.

Particularly significant is the propulsion system. The LRLACM is powered by the indigenous Manik small turbofan engine (STFE) — a 400-kgf thrust-class turbofan developed by the Gas Turbine Research Establishment (GTRE). Previous-generation Nirbhay cruise missiles had used the Russian NPO Saturn 36MT engine; the transition to the Manik engine, achieved over the course of the Indigenous Technology Cruise Missile (ITCM) programme that culminated in successful tests from February 2023 onwards, was the critical step in eliminating foreign-supply dependency in the subsonic cruise missile category. By design, the LRLACM is intended to achieve substantively complete indigenous content across structures, propulsion, avionics, guidance, and seekers.

The Manik engine is described by senior DRDO officials, including a former Chairman, as more economical to operate than the earlier Russian engine. It is designed as an expendable turbofan — built for the operational economics of a cruise missile that completes a single flight to target, rather than a reusable aircraft engine.

## The lineage: Nirbhay → ITCM → LRLACM

The LRLACM is the third major iteration of a subsonic cruise missile family that DRDO has been developing for more than a decade. The first was the Nirbhay missile — India's first long-range subsonic cruise missile, with development beginning in the early 2000s and the first flight test conducted in 2013. The Nirbhay, with a range of approximately 1,000 kilometres, has been tested across multiple iterations and configurations and is reportedly already in limited deployment with the Strategic Forces Command.

The second iteration was the Indigenous Technology Cruise Missile (ITCM) — a technology-demonstrator programme initiated to validate the indigenous Manik turbofan engine and to achieve substantially complete indigenisation of the subsonic cruise missile system. After several developmental setbacks during 2020-22, ITCM achieved a successful test flight on 21 February 2023, validating the indigenous engine and clearing the path for the more substantial extended-range variant.

The LRLACM is the third generation — a longer-range, more capable iteration that draws on the indigenisation work of ITCM and extends the operational range from Nirbhay's ~1,000 kilometres to approximately 1,500 kilometres. The Acceptance of Necessity for the LRLACM was granted by the Defence Acquisition Council in August 2023; the maiden flight test was conducted on 12 November 2024 from a mobile articulated launcher at ITR Chandipur. Monday's test, from Abdul Kalam Island, was conducted in the missile's full operational configuration and represents a substantive maturity milestone on the path to formal induction.

## What 1,500 kilometres delivers

A range of approximately 1,500 kilometres places the LRLACM in the category of long-range cruise missiles. From Indian launch positions in the western theatre, it can cover deep targets in Pakistan and into Afghanistan and parts of the Iranian plateau. From the northern theatre, it places much of the Tibetan plateau and Chinese installations in Xinjiang within reach. From naval platforms operating in the Bay of Bengal, the Arabian Sea, or the Indian Ocean, it provides the Indian Navy with a substantial stand-off precision-strike capability — meaning the ship that fires the missile can remain well outside the range of land-based defensive systems.

The combination of a 1,500-km range with a terrain-hugging, low-altitude subsonic flight profile gives the LRLACM a defining tactical attribute: it is difficult to detect, track, and intercept using conventional radar-based air defence. Subsonic missiles are slower than supersonic systems like BrahMos, but the difficulty of detection at terrain-hugging altitudes — where radar horizons are short and ground clutter complicates target discrimination — substantially compensates for the lower speed. This is the same operational philosophy that underpins the US Tomahawk and Russian Kalibr families of cruise missiles.

## Where it fits in India's arsenal

The LRLACM does not replace existing capabilities in India's missile inventory; it complements them. The BrahMos cruise missile, jointly developed with Russia and now produced in increasing variants in India, provides supersonic (Mach 2.8-3.0) speed but at shorter ranges. The Pralay tactical ballistic missile addresses shorter-range, faster-time-to-target requirements. The Agni family of ballistic missiles addresses the longer-range nuclear deterrent requirements. The Shaurya provides another point on the strategic curve.

The LRLACM, in this architecture, addresses a specific gap: long-range, all-weather, low-altitude precision strike with a difficult-to-intercept flight profile, deliverable from multiple platforms, with substantially complete indigenous content. It is the kind of capability that, in modern conventional warfare, has acquired growing strategic value — the experience of contemporary conflicts has reinforced the operational utility of cruise-missile capabilities of this kind.

The LRLACM is, additionally, expected to be a central asset of the new Integrated Rocket Force that the Government of India has approved for establishment. The Rocket Force concept envisages an integrated tri-service command structure for India's various missile systems — bringing the LRLACM, BrahMos, Pralay, Shaurya, and other systems under unified command rather than the current service-distributed arrangement. The conceptual model draws partial inspiration from the People's Liberation Army Rocket Force (PLARF) of China, which has been one of the most consequential elements of Chinese military modernisation over the past decade.

## The international comparison

The LRLACM places India in the small group of countries with indigenously developed, multi-platform, long-range subsonic cruise missiles. The United States operates the Tomahawk family — first deployed in the 1980s and continuously upgraded through multiple block variants, with a range that has been extended in some variants beyond 2,500 kilometres. Russia operates the Kalibr (Club for export markets) family, used in active combat in Syria and Ukraine, with a comparable range envelope to the LRLACM. China operates the DH-10 and CJ-10 family. The United Kingdom and France co-produce the Storm Shadow / SCALP-EG, a slightly different class but with overlapping operational applications.

India's entry into this category — with substantially indigenous design, propulsion, and electronics — represents a meaningful capability accretion. It also represents the closing of a long-standing technology gap in the Indian defence-industrial base. The completion of indigenous turbofan engine technology, in particular, addresses a category of foreign-supply dependency that has historically constrained Indian missile development.

## The broader DRDO testing tempo

Monday's LRLACM test sits within a notably active recent period for DRDO. Earlier in the same week, on 13 June 2026, DRDO successfully demonstrated several key technologies aimed at strengthening India's defence capabilities against various types of enemy threats — including three consecutive tests of next-generation ballistic missile defence (BMD) interceptors. Across the past 18 months, DRDO has conducted the maiden flight test of India's first long-range hypersonic missile (November 2024, also from Abdul Kalam Island), successful trials of the RudraM-II air-to-surface missile in partnership with the Indian Air Force, the maiden LRLACM test in November 2024, and a series of other systems-level demonstrations.

The aggregate pattern reflects what defence analysts have characterised as a maturation phase in Indian strategic weapon system development — with multiple programmes simultaneously reaching the testing and validation stage. This is, in turn, the consequence of substantial sustained R&D investment over the past 15-20 years.

## The road ahead

For the LRLACM, the immediate horizon will involve additional developmental and user-trial flights to validate the full performance envelope, the multi-platform launch configurations, and the various seeker and terminal guidance options. The progression from successful technical demonstrator tests through user trials, to limited series production, and finally to full induction is typically a multi-year process. Defence Ministry statements have indicated that induction proceedings will be initiated once the user trials are complete.

For the Indian armed forces, induction of the LRLACM will be a substantive enhancement of long-range, precision-strike capability across all three services. For India's defence-industrial base, the LRLACM programme is also a meaningful test of the Aatmanirbhar Bharat framework — the policy push to substitute foreign-sourced defence equipment with domestically designed and manufactured alternatives. The LRLACM, with its indigenous Manik engine, Indian-designed avionics, and Indian-manufactured airframe and subsystems, is one of the most complete realisations of that framework in the strategic weapon systems category.

For now, what Monday's test from Abdul Kalam Island establishes is that the system works — that the propulsion, guidance, control, navigation, and terminal accuracy of the LRLACM in its full operational configuration meet the design specifications. The harder work, of moving from successful test to deployed operational asset, lies ahead. But the technical foundation, on the evidence of the past 18 months of testing, is now substantively in place.
