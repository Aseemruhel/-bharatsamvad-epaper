LEAD: Union Home and Cooperation Minister Amit Shah visited the G-7 Border Out Post near Bhuj in Gujarat and interacted with Border Security Force personnel deployed along the India-Pakistan border. During the visit, he inaugurated the G-7 and G-13 Border Out Posts and highlighted the government’s commitment to strengthening India’s border infrastructure, improving facilities for jawans and building a modern, technology-backed security grid.

The programme was attended by Gujarat Chief Minister Bhupendra Patel, Deputy Chief Minister Harsh Sanghavi, the Union Home Secretary, the Director of the Intelligence Bureau, the Secretary for Border Management, the Director General of the BSF and other senior officials. The visit carried both symbolic and strategic significance, as it took place in a sensitive frontier region that includes difficult terrain such as Sir Creek, Harami Nala and the desert stretches of Kutch.

## Salute to the first line of defence

Addressing BSF personnel, Shah said the force has carried the responsibility of guarding two of India’s most challenging borders over the last six decades. He noted that BSF personnel serve in extremely difficult climatic and geographical conditions, from freezing temperatures of minus 45 degrees Celsius to scorching heat above 45 degrees Celsius.

He said that the courage and discipline of the BSF allow citizens across the country to live peacefully. More than 2,000 BSF personnel have made the supreme sacrifice in the service of the nation, and their contribution continues to inspire public respect for the force.

## G-7 and G-13 as symbols of stronger infrastructure

The newly inaugurated G-7 and G-13 outposts were described as examples of determined engineering in difficult terrain. In areas where the geography makes routine movement, water supply, medical access and construction extremely challenging, the BSF has developed a stronger security base with improved facilities.

Shah said that the government is focused on providing watch towers, connecting roads, drinking water, medical support, better living arrangements and modern fencing. These steps are expected to reduce hardship for personnel and improve operational readiness.

## From border security to territorial security

A major theme of the address was the shift from a narrow idea of “border security” to a wider model of “territorial security.” Shah said that in the coming years, India will move toward a four-sided security grid involving citizens, civil administration, local police, the military and BSF personnel.

This approach aims to create a more coordinated and responsive security environment in frontier areas. By connecting local information, administrative support, military preparedness and BSF vigilance, the government seeks to strengthen security beyond the immediate border fence.

## Technology-led protection of sensitive areas

The Union Home Minister said the government is working to build a “leak-proof” security grid. Where physical fencing is difficult because of rivers, marshy ground or other geographical challenges, technical fencing and surveillance systems are being introduced.

Drones, radars, modern watch towers and advanced monitoring systems are expected to support BSF personnel in detecting and preventing threats. Shah expressed confidence that such a modern grid would make it extremely difficult for hostile elements to breach the frontier.

## Special focus on Sir Creek and Harami Nala

Shah referred to Sir Creek and Harami Nala as areas that were once considered sensitive from the security point of view. He said the government has begun strengthening this belt through watch towers, roads, drinking water arrangements, medical facilities, housing support and new fencing.

He added that with continued work over the next two years, this region is expected to become permanently secure against hostile designs. The message was clear: border infrastructure is now being treated as a core part of national security planning.

## Public awareness about BSF’s role

The Home Minister also mentioned a centre in Banaskantha where people can learn about the difficult duties performed by BSF personnel. According to him, more than 2.5 lakh people have visited the centre in about a month, gaining a better understanding of the force’s responsibilities.

Feedback from visitors, especially mothers who said they would feel proud if their children joined the BSF, reflected growing public appreciation for the force. Such initiatives help bring the sacrifices and daily discipline of border personnel closer to ordinary citizens.

## Strengthening the future of border management

Shah said the government is also considering adding more areas under the BSF’s operational responsibility where required. He noted that unfinished fencing in some border regions has remained a challenge, but efforts are being made to complete physical fencing where possible and introduce technical fencing in riverine, forested and difficult areas.

The larger goal is to build a seamless security architecture that combines infrastructure, technology, manpower and local coordination. For the personnel deployed on the front line, this also means better facilities and improved support systems.

## A message of confidence from the frontier

The Bhuj visit underlined the government’s belief that national security depends not only on weapons and surveillance but also on the morale of the personnel who stand guard in harsh conditions. By inaugurating new outposts and speaking directly with jawans, the Home Minister sought to reinforce the message that the country stands firmly behind its first line of defence.

For Bharat Samvad readers, the story is also a reminder that border development is no longer only an administrative task. It is a strategic national project that protects citizens, supports soldiers and strengthens India’s confidence along its frontiers.
