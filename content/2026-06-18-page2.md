LEAD: India's Northeast region — the eight states of Arunachal Pradesh, Assam, Manipur, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland, Tripura and Sikkim — is in the middle of the most consequential connectivity transformation in its modern history. Railway lines that took decades to plan are now reaching state capitals for the first time. National Highway networks built at a pace of nearly a thousand kilometres a year are linking border districts to mainland India with all-weather all-season access. New greenfield airports are extending civil aviation to states that had only one or two facilities a decade ago. And major bridges across the Brahmaputra and other Northeastern rivers are knitting the region's previously isolated geographies into an integrated economic landscape.

Behind this transformation is a substantial, sustained, multi-decade public investment framework — anchored by the Act East Policy, the North East Special Infrastructure Development Scheme (NESIDS), the PM Development Initiative for North East Region (PM-DevINE) and the Uttar Poorva Transformative Industrialisation Scheme (UNNATI). The cumulative effect is that the Northeast is being decisively repositioned in India's economic geography — from frontier to gateway, from periphery to pivot.

## Railways: capitals being connected for the first time

The most visible single thread in the Northeast's connectivity story is the railway build-out. The Government's annual railway budget allocation for Assam and the broader Northeast region has risen more than fivefold — from approximately Rs 2,122 crore per year during 2009-14 to Rs 11,486 crore in the current 2026-27 financial year. Total ongoing railway projects in the region now stand at approximately Rs 72,468 crore. These are the largest railway investments the Northeast has ever received.

The headline achievements of the past 18 months are substantial. In September 2025, the 51.38-kilometre Bairabi-Sairang line in Mizoram became operational — bringing the first passenger train service in independent India's history to the state of Mizoram. The state's residents, who for decades had relied on road transport into and out of the region, can now board a train within their own state for the first time. The line connects Bairabi in Kolasib district to Sairang, near the state capital Aizawl, and integrates Mizoram into the Indian Railways network.

In Manipur, the 110.63-kilometre Jiribam-Imphal broad-gauge line — a feat of engineering through deeply hilly and forested terrain involving multiple tunnels and viaducts — is approaching completion. Project officials have indicated likely completion of the full line by December 2026, with the Khongsang-Awangkhul stretch having reached significant milestones. The Jiribam-Imphal line will bring rail connectivity to Imphal, the capital of Manipur, for the first time, integrating the state into the national rail network in a way that previous decades had not achieved.

Nagaland is similarly on the verge of a historic railway breakthrough. The Molvom-Pherima section of approximately 14.09 kilometres is scheduled to open by October 2026; the subsequent Pherima-Zubza section of approximately 37.57 kilometres is scheduled for December 2029, which will finally bring rail connectivity to Kohima, the capital of Nagaland. Tetelia-Byrnihat (22 km), connecting Assam to Meghalaya at Byrnihat, is also on track for completion by December 2026.

In Arunachal Pradesh, the Murkongselek-Pasighat line is under active construction, with the Sille-Pasighat section of approximately 10.55 kilometres scheduled for completion in 2026. Final Location Surveys have also been completed for new railway lines to Tawang, Pasighat-Parashuram Kund-Wakro, and Bame-Aalo-Mechuka — extending the railway map further into Arunachal Pradesh's strategic frontier districts.

Tripura, in a separate achievement, has become the first state in the Northeast to achieve 100 per cent railway electrification. The Agartala-Sabroom line, extending the rail network to the southernmost part of Tripura close to the Bangladesh border, has been progressively brought into service since 2016.

## Highways: 16,000 kilometres of new arteries

The road network expansion in the Northeast has been equally consequential. Between 2014 and 2024, nearly 10,000 kilometres of national highways were constructed in the Northeast at a cost of approximately Rs 1.07 lakh crore. By July 2025, the total national highway length constructed in the Northeast Region had reached approximately 16,207 kilometres. A further 5,000 kilometres of highway projects are currently under implementation.

The impact on travel times has been transformative. The Guwahati-Imphal corridor, which until recently took approximately 13 hours by road, can now be covered in under 8 hours along upgraded national highway sections. Similar reductions have been achieved across multiple inter-state and inter-district corridors throughout the region.

The Arunachal Frontier Highway — currently under construction — will, when completed, provide all-weather all-season road access across Arunachal Pradesh's border districts, transforming what was historically among India's least accessible territories into a connected and integrated economic corridor. The Sela Tunnel, opened in early 2024, provides year-round connectivity to Tawang district — a stretch that was historically inaccessible during heavy snowfall periods. The Nechiphu Tunnel complements the Sela in providing redundant all-weather access. These two tunnels together have substantially altered the operational geography of access to Tawang, Arunachal Pradesh's strategically and culturally important district adjoining the China border.

## Bridges: the longest river crossings in India

Major bridge projects are knitting together the river-divided geography of the Northeast — particularly in Assam, where the Brahmaputra River has historically separated districts and constrained logistics. The Bogibeel Bridge, a 4.94-kilometre rail-cum-road bridge across the Brahmaputra, has been operational since 2018 and has substantially improved north-south connectivity in upper Assam. The Dhubri-Phulbari Bridge — under construction across the Brahmaputra in lower Assam — will, on completion, be at 19.3 kilometres the longest river bridge in India. The bridge is scheduled for completion in 2028.

In Arunachal Pradesh, the Dhola-Sadiya Bridge — better known as the Bhupen Hazarika Setu — at 9.15 kilometres remains India's second-longest river bridge and provides critical connectivity into the easternmost districts of the state. Together with the Bogibeel Bridge and the Dhubri-Phulbari Bridge under construction, these structures are transforming the river-crossing economics of Northeast logistics.

## Aviation: ten new greenfield airports

The aviation dimension of the Northeast's connectivity transformation has been substantial. Ten new greenfield airports have been developed across the region since 2014 — particularly transforming air access in Arunachal Pradesh, Mizoram and Nagaland, three of the states with historically thinnest aviation infrastructure. Each new airport in the Northeast typically becomes the catalyst for tourism flows, business travel, medical evacuation capability, and emergency response — all of which the region had previously lacked.

Shillong's Umroi Airport in Meghalaya is being upgraded to handle wide-body operations by 2027, ending the state's dependence on Guwahati Airport for its 3.96 million residents. Agartala's Maharaja Bir Bikram Airport in Tripura has been expanded and modernised. New airports across Arunachal Pradesh — including the operational Donyi Polo Airport at Itanagar (the state's first greenfield airport) — have opened the state to air access in a fundamentally new way.

The Union Government has also expanded emergency landing facilities on national highways in the region — strengthening defence preparedness and disaster response. One such facility was inaugurated at Moran near Dibrugarh, recognising the operational geography of a region in which civilian and strategic infrastructure necessarily overlap.

## The cross-border connectivity dimension

Beyond domestic connectivity, the Northeast is becoming India's principal gateway for cross-border land connectivity to Southeast Asia. The India-Myanmar-Thailand Trilateral Highway, when fully operational, will provide a direct road link from India through Myanmar to Thailand — opening trade and travel possibilities that previous generations of Indians could not access without going around through other countries.

The Kaladan Multi-Modal Transit Transport Project — a strategic initiative integrating sea, river and road transport — connects Kolkata Port with the Northeast via Sittwe Port in Myanmar and Paletwa river port, bypassing the Siliguri Corridor and providing an alternative route into Mizoram via Lawngtlai. The project has the potential to reduce transit times by up to four days for goods entering the Northeast from the Indian east coast.

These projects, together with India's continued engagement with BIMSTEC (Bay of Bengal Initiative for Multi-Sectoral Technical and Economic Cooperation) and the BBIN (Bangladesh-Bhutan-India-Nepal) Motor Vehicles Agreement framework, position the Northeast not as a remote borderland but as the geographic centre of India's economic and strategic engagement with Southeast Asia and the Bay of Bengal littoral.

## The investment dimension

The connectivity build-out is being matched by parallel investment-promotion efforts. The Advantage Assam 2.0 summit, held in February 2025, brought leading global investors to the region and positioned Assam — and the Northeast as a whole — as a major emerging investment destination. The summit's tagline, "Advantage Assam: India's expressway to ASEAN," captured the conceptual repositioning of the region.

The UNNATI (Uttar Poorva Transformative Industrialisation) scheme, with an allocation of Rs 10,037 crore spread over 10 years, provides the structural incentive framework for industrial development in the region. The PM-DevINE (PM Development Initiative for North East Region) scheme has received approximately Rs 2,300 crore in the current 2026-27 Union Budget, supporting infrastructure, livelihoods and convergence programmes across the eight Northeastern states.

The combined gross state domestic product (GSDP) of the eight Northeastern states has grown at over 7 per cent annually in the post-COVID years — a figure that, given the region's historic constraints, represents substantial economic momentum. The combination of better connectivity, targeted investment incentives, and growing private-sector confidence has produced a virtuous cycle that is now visible across multiple sectors.

## Tourism: the next horizon

The Northeast's natural and cultural endowment — among the most distinctive in India — is now beginning to be matched by the connectivity that enables tourism to scale. Meghalaya draws approximately 1.2 million annual visitors to Cherrapunji's living root bridges, limestone caves and waterfall landscapes. Sikkim's Himalayan tourism continues to grow. Arunachal Pradesh's Tawang — now accessible year-round through the Sela Tunnel — is opening to higher visitor volumes than was previously possible. Manipur, Mizoram, Nagaland and Tripura are each developing their own tourism profiles around festivals, heritage, biodiversity and adventure tourism.

Tourism, in particular, is a sector in which improved connectivity translates directly into economic outcomes for local communities. Every new road, every new railway station, every new airport, and every new bridge in the Northeast has a downstream multiplier effect on tourism revenues, hospitality employment, and the wider services economy that supports a visitor flow.

## The Prime Minister's commitment

Prime Minister Narendra Modi has made the Northeast a sustained priority of his administration. In the first three months of 2026 alone, the Prime Minister has visited the region multiple times and has inaugurated projects worth over Rs 47,600 crore — including a high-speed corridor, a wildlife-protecting elevated highway, and the distribution of land ownership certificates to nearly 3.5 lakh tea garden workers in a historic welfare measure. The pace of high-level engagement signals the political and policy weight that the region now carries in national priorities.

Union Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman, in successive budget statements, has emphasised that the Northeast is given top priority in infrastructure, investment and skill development — a stated priority that is reflected in the substantial budget allocations across multiple Central schemes targeting the region.

## What it adds up to

Taken together, the Rs 72,468 crore of ongoing railway projects, the 16,207 kilometres of constructed national highways with 5,000 more under construction, the 10 new greenfield airports, the major bridge projects across the Brahmaputra and other rivers, the Sela and Nechiphu tunnels providing year-round access to Tawang, the UNNATI, PM-DevINE and NESIDS schemes providing structural investment incentive, and the Act East Policy framework positioning the region as a gateway to Southeast Asia — collectively describe a Northeast region that is being reshaped in fundamental and durable ways.

For the residents of the eight Northeastern states, the practical effect of this transformation is being felt in measurable terms: shorter travel times, more reliable connections to mainland India, growing tourism and investment flows, new employment opportunities, and the steady normalisation of access to services and infrastructure that other Indians had taken for granted.

## The road ahead

The immediate horizon will be defined by the operational launches of multiple major projects: the Jiribam-Imphal line in Manipur (December 2026), the Tetelia-Byrnihat line connecting Assam and Meghalaya (December 2026), the Murkongselek-Pasighat extension in Arunachal Pradesh, the Molvom-Pherima section in Nagaland, and the continued progress on the Dhubri-Phulbari Bridge with its scheduled 2028 completion.

The longer horizon — toward 2029 and beyond — points to a Northeast that will be substantially better integrated with the national infrastructure network than it has been at any point since Independence. The connectivity decade, in many ways, is closing the gap that decades of geographic isolation had created.

For now, on the morning of 16 June 2026, what stands is a region in the middle of its most consequential infrastructure transformation since Independence, with the Act East Policy framework providing the strategic envelope and the visible projects on the ground delivering the substantive change. The Northeast is no longer a frontier of India's development map — it is becoming one of its central stages.
