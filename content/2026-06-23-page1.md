LEAD: It is a contradiction that has, in the past three weeks, become impossible to disguise. On Sunday, 21 June 2026, Field Marshal Asim Munir, Chief of Defence Forces of Pakistan, stood at the Bürgenstock luxury resort overlooking Lake Lucerne in Switzerland — co-anchoring, alongside Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif, US Vice President JD Vance, and Qatar's Prime Minister Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani, the quadrilateral meeting that crystallised the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding to end the 2026 Iran war. Four days earlier, on 17 June, President Donald Trump had signed the same memorandum during a dinner with French President Emmanuel Macron at the Palace of Versailles, following the conclusion of the G7 summit. The Iranian President, Masoud Pezeshkian, had signed his copy in Tehran. The Memorandum, brokered primarily by Pakistan with Qatar, Saudi Arabia, Turkey and Egypt as facilitating partners, marks one of the most consequential outcomes of Pakistan's recent diplomatic activity. The White House has formally praised "Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and Field Marshal Asim Munir" for being "helpful mediators." The Diplomat has labelled Munir "Trump's favorite Field Marshal." Pakistan, the international optics suggest, is on the rise.

The optics are an illusion. While Munir collected mediator credentials in Switzerland, Pakistan's own provinces of Balochistan and Khyber Pakhtunkhwa continued to burn. The country sits, by independent measure, atop the 2026 Global Terrorism Index — the most terrorism-affected nation on the planet. Balochistan recorded 254 terrorist attacks in 2025, a 26 per cent year-on-year increase, with more than 400 deaths recorded in the province alone. Year 2025 emerged as the deadliest in Pakistan's history of internal conflict — with 2,115 militants, 664 security personnel, and 580 civilians killed across the country, according to data from the Pakistan Institute for Conflict and Security Studies. The Afghanistan-Pakistan war that began in October 2025 grinds into its ninth month, with no negotiated end in sight. Foreign Direct Investment, the lifeblood of the country's struggling economy, has collapsed to USD 808 million in the first half of fiscal 2026 — barely half of the USD 1.425 billion received in the corresponding period a year earlier. Intelligence sources describe troop morale on the western frontier as the lowest in recent history. The country's most ambitious bilateral economic relationship — the China-Pakistan Economic Corridor (CPEC) — is, by Chinese assessment, struggling under the weight of repeated targeted attacks on Chinese personnel and infrastructure.

What unites this picture is not bad luck. It is structural failure. And the more energy Munir invests in burnishing Pakistan's foreign image, the more visible the gap becomes between the country he wants the world to see and the country his own citizens actually inhabit.

## The Balochistan front: Operation Herof 2.0 and what came after

The first of Pakistan's two active insurgencies is in the southwestern province of Balochistan, where the Balochistan Liberation Army (BLA) has been waging an armed campaign for independence with growing operational sophistication. The watershed moment of the current phase came at dawn on 30 January 2026, when the BLA — under its leader Bashir Zeb — launched what it called "Operation Herof 2.0" (Operation Black Storm 2.0) across at least nine districts of Balochistan simultaneously. Quetta, Gwadar, Mastung, Nushki, Pasni, Kharan, Kalat — town after town reported coordinated armed assaults, suicide bombings, and attacks on government installations.

The most consequential single battle was at Nushki — a town of approximately 50,000 inhabitants, where BLA fighters seized control of six administrative buildings, including the police lines, the judicial complex, the deputy commissioner's office, and a camp of the Federal Constabulary. Pakistan deployed helicopters and drones; the battle to recapture Nushki lasted three full days. Seven police officers were killed during the engagement. The BLA released videos of the operation showing, among the participants, female fighters in combat — a deliberate signal about the breadth of social mobilisation the insurgency is drawing from in the Baloch population.

By 5 February 2026, when clearance operations were declared complete, the Pakistani military reported 216 militants killed, 22 security personnel killed, and 36 civilians caught in the crossfire. The BLA contested these numbers but did not deny the scale of the engagement. In subsequent weeks, attacks continued. On 11 March 2025, the BLA had executed the hijacking of the Jaffar Express — a coordinated armed assault on a passenger train passing through Bolan that left more than 20 dead and dramatised the BLA's ability to strike at the deepest infrastructure of the Pakistani state. The Zaffar Express bombing of recent weeks has repeated that signalling, executed against security personnel travelling aboard.

## The Khyber Pakhtunkhwa front: nine months of cross-border war

The second of Pakistan's two active insurgencies is in the northwestern province of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, where the Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan (TTP) — the Pakistani Taliban movement, distinct from but ideologically aligned with the Afghan Taliban — operates across both sides of the Durand Line, the Pakistan-Afghanistan border that the Afghan side has never formally recognised. The current phase escalated dramatically in October 2025, when Pakistan carried out an airstrike in Kabul that it claimed targeted the leader of the TTP. The Afghan Taliban responded with cross-border attacks, opening what has become the 2026 Afghanistan-Pakistan war.

The conflict has continued through nine consecutive months. Pakistani Defence Minister Khawaja Asif issued multiple warnings — most prominently on 11 February 2026 — that Pakistan would escalate military action against militant groups operating from Afghan territory if the Taliban regime did not act. The escalation came on the night of 21 February 2026, with Pakistani airstrikes in the Afghan provinces of Nangarhar, Paktika and Khost. The Taliban responded with airstrikes claimed against Pakistani military positions in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and Balochistan, alongside ground engagements at multiple border crossings — Torkham, Nazyan, Achin, and across Zabul, Kandahar, Khost, Kunar.

Through the spring and into the summer, the engagements have continued. Drone strikes, mortar exchanges, missile incidents, border infiltrations, and the steady accumulation of civilian and military casualties have become a recurring feature of life along the Pakistan-Afghanistan frontier. The Pakistani state has been progressively forced to acknowledge what it spent years denying: that the same Taliban regime in Kabul whose 2021 return to power was facilitated by the Pakistani security establishment is now Pakistan's most dangerous adversary. The 1990s-era thesis — that strategic depth through proxy management would secure Pakistan's western frontier in perpetuity — has comprehensively collapsed.

## The Frankenstein observation

This is the observation that India's First Secretary at the United Nations, Anupama Singh, captured at the 62nd Session of the UN Human Rights Council in Geneva on 18 June — exactly three days before Munir's Bürgenstock appearance. Pakistan, Ms Singh said, is "a living example of a Frankenstein state, which is shocked when its own monster bites back." The phrase, which subsequently dominated international coverage of the statement, captured something genuine about the structural pathology that underlies both of Pakistan's insurgencies. The TTP that today wages war on the Pakistani state was nurtured by the Pakistani security establishment over decades. The Afghan Taliban that today hosts and shelters the TTP was facilitated into power by the Pakistani security establishment. The strategic-proxy doctrine that produced these arrangements was not an accident or a miscalculation; it was a sustained, deliberate, institutional policy across decades.

The Balochistan insurgency operates on a different historical track — its grievances are rooted in the unresolved consequences of the 1948 forcible accession of Balochistan to Pakistan, in seven decades of resource extraction without local benefit, in the chronic denial of political autonomy, and in the steady militarisation of the provincial state. But the pattern is structurally similar: a Pakistani security establishment that has consistently preferred coercion over accommodation, repression over reform, and the cultivation of compliance over the resolution of legitimate political demand. When the Anupama Singh formulation referenced "denial of basic freedoms" and demands for "bread, electricity, rights, and dignity" being met with "bullets and brutality" — the reference was to documented patterns of state violence across both Pakistan-occupied Jammu and Kashmir and Balochistan, with the May-June 2026 Rawalakot crackdown and the Joint Awami Action Committee long march providing the most recent visible illustrations.

## The Versailles moment, the Bürgenstock moment

Set against this domestic picture, the foreign optics that Munir has accumulated over recent weeks acquire a particular character. On 17 June, the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding to end the 2026 Iran war — formally the Islamabad Memorandum of Understanding between the United States of America and the Islamic Republic of Iran — was signed remotely by President Trump and President Pezeshkian. The Trump signature occurred at Versailles, during a dinner with President Macron following the G7. The Pezeshkian signature occurred in Tehran. The Memorandum provides for a 60-day extension of the existing ceasefire to negotiate final terms.

The Pakistani mediation that produced the Memorandum was led by Prime Minister Sharif, Field Marshal Munir, and Deputy Prime Minister and Foreign Minister Ishaq Dar, with Qatari Prime Minister Sheikh Mohammed bin Abdulrahman Al Thani as the principal co-mediator. The Diplomat has, in its assessment of Munir's recent activity, described him as "the main driving force behind Pakistan's image overhaul." The Fox News coverage of his role describes him as having "emerged as a key intermediary in negotiations aimed at preventing renewed conflict with Iran, placing Pakistan back at the center of U.S. diplomacy in the Middle East." The 21 June Bürgenstock meeting — formally a US-Iran quadrilateral with Pakistan and Qatar as anchoring mediators — saw Munir alongside Vice President Vance, Prime Minister Sharif, Prime Minister Sheikh Mohammed of Qatar, and senior representatives of both belligerent parties.

These are not nothing. Pakistan's positioning at the centre of the Iran-US diplomatic architecture is, in narrow terms, a substantive achievement of opportunistic diplomacy. Munir's institutional capacity to translate his constitutional position into international currency is real. He has used the Pakistan-Saudi Strategic Mutual Defense Pact, the Pakistan-Iran relationship, the Pakistan-China relationship, and the renewed Trump-era US engagement to construct, at considerable speed, a Middle Eastern diplomatic platform that places Pakistan adjacent to the most consequential conversations of the moment.

## The contradiction explained

The question worth asking is: how should this be read? Is Munir's foreign positioning genuine strategic achievement, building durable international stature for Pakistan? Or is it precisely the kind of optics-heavy, substance-light projection that mid-sized powers use when their domestic position becomes increasingly untenable?

The honest analytical answer is that it is much closer to the second than the first. Three structural realities make this clear.

First, the contradiction between Pakistan's claimed regional security role and its actual inability to provide internal security is now visible to every serious observer. As Maryam Shaikh, the South Asia analyst whose verdict heads this column, put it: "The pretense that it can be a so-called net security provider when it can't provide security at home fools nobody." A country that has lost three-day battles in its own provincial towns, that has fought a nine-month border war against the very regime it helped install, that sits at the top of the Global Terrorism Index, cannot credibly position itself as a guarantor of international peace.

Second, the diplomatic positioning is heavily personality-dependent and externally contingent. The Trump administration has invested in Munir for specific tactical reasons related to its Iran policy. That investment can be withdrawn — and historically has been withdrawn from successive Pakistani leaders when their utility has diminished. The Pakistani diplomatic platform that looks impressive in mid-2026 is built on inherently transient external interests. When those interests shift, the platform shifts with them.

Third, the costs of the foreign positioning are being absorbed domestically. The very Field Marshal who should be coordinating the response to the Balochistan insurgency and the Afghan border war is, instead, accumulating air miles to Tehran, Beijing, Riyadh and Switzerland. The very institutional capacity that should be addressing CPEC security failures is being redirected to mediation theatre. The intelligence sources cited by News18 in May 2026 were explicit: Munir's "aggressive posturing increasingly appears less a demonstration of sovereign authority and more a defensive counter-narrative from a leadership running out of answers."

## The blame-India tactic

A recurring tactic in Munir's domestic communication has been the attribution of both insurgencies to Indian sponsorship. During his Eid-ul-Azha visit to frontline troops in Zhob and Quetta, Munir accused India of "actively promoting and sponsoring terrorism inside Pakistan" and vowed to pursue "terror facilitators with full force." Similar attributions have followed each major attack. The Pakistani military's framing of the BLA's Operation Herof 2.0 was that it was Indian-sponsored.

This is, by any responsible standard of evidence, untrue. The Balochistan insurgency has structural and historical roots that long pre-date the current phase of India-Pakistan tensions. The BLA's organisational origins go back decades, with grievances rooted in the post-Partition handling of Kalat State, the Bhutto-era 1973-77 military operations, the Musharraf-era 2006 killing of Akbar Bugti, and the unresolved political demands of the Baloch population. The TTP insurgency has equally Pakistani-internal origins, traceable directly to the policies of the Pakistani security establishment in the 1980s and 1990s. The Afghan Taliban whose return to power has now turned against Pakistan was, comprehensively and on the documented record, facilitated by the Pakistani security establishment itself.

India's Ministry of External Affairs has, with growing sharpness, rejected the Munir framing. When Pakistan accused India of involvement in the February 2026 attacks, MEA spokesperson Randhir Jaiswal said: "Instead of parroting frivolous claims each time there is a violent incident, it would do better to focus on addressing long-standing demands of its people in the region." Ms Anupama Singh's UN statement on 18 June extended that framing to the most prestigious multilateral forum — and gave it the "Frankenstein state" formulation that has now entered international diplomatic vocabulary.

## The economic translation

The strategic picture has direct economic consequences. Foreign Direct Investment into Pakistan in the first half of fiscal 2026 collapsed to USD 808 million — barely 57 per cent of the USD 1.425 billion received in the corresponding period a year earlier. Imtiaz Gul, executive director of the Centre for Research and Security Studies in Islamabad, put the diagnosis directly: "No sane national or international investor will risk their money in an extremely volatile situation." The crisis, Mr Gul observed, "is rooted in problems centred in the province itself and linked to Islamabad's approach."

The CPEC framework, around which Pakistan's economic strategy has been built for over a decade, is facing its most serious challenge in years. Chinese personnel have repeatedly been targeted by Baloch insurgents; Chinese-built infrastructure has come under attack; Beijing's confidence in Pakistan's ability to secure CPEC assets is, according to intelligence assessments, at its lowest point. The combination of stalled CPEC, collapsed FDI, persistent currency pressures, and IMF dependence creates an economic backdrop that is the opposite of the prosperity-driven international stature that Munir's optics suggest.

## What the contradiction signifies

For India, the contradiction is consequential in multiple respects. It validates the analytical framing that India has progressively been deploying in international forums — most sharply through Ms Anupama Singh's "Frankenstein state" statement at Geneva on 18 June. It exposes the credibility gap between Munir's claimed role as regional security provider and Pakistan's actual security capability. It clarifies the strategic environment in which India's diplomatic engagement with Pakistan is unfolding — one in which the Pakistani state is structurally weaker than its rhetorical positioning suggests.

For the region, the picture has wider implications. Pakistan's progressive inability to control its own western frontier creates substantial security externalities for Afghanistan, for Iran (whose Sistan-Baluchestan province shares the Baloch insurgency dynamic), and for the broader Central Asian space. A Pakistan that cannot provide its own internal security cannot be a credible partner in any regional security architecture — whatever optics its Field Marshal may project at international forums.

For the international community, the contradiction is now visible to anyone who reads beyond the photo-opportunities. The Diplomat's characterisation of Munir as "Trump's favorite Field Marshal" carries within it the implicit observation that Pakistan's current diplomatic positioning rests on a particular bilateral relationship that can shift with administrations, with policy reversals, or with the changing tactical needs of the moment. Few serious strategic analyses of Pakistan's medium-term trajectory today extrapolate the present diplomatic optics into durable strategic position.

## What lies ahead

In the coming weeks and months, multiple trajectories of the contradiction will continue to develop. The Iran-US 60-day negotiation window will either succeed in producing the final text of a peace deal — in which case Pakistan's mediator role will receive a one-time stature boost — or it will collapse, with attendant consequences for Munir's claimed achievement. The Afghanistan-Pakistan war will continue to grind on without obvious off-ramps, with the cross-border dynamic continuing to drain Pakistani military resources and credibility. The Balochistan insurgency will continue to escalate in operational sophistication, with the BLA's demonstrated ability to seize district towns and execute coordinated multi-front operations marking it among the more capable insurgencies globally. The CPEC question with China will continue to fester. The FDI picture will, on current indicators, continue to deteriorate.

For Pakistan's actual citizens — the residents of Quetta and Gwadar living under successive states of emergency, the families in Bannu and Lakki Marwat absorbing the cost of the Afghan war, the people of Rawalakot demanding bread and electricity from a state that responds with bullets, the families in Karachi and Lahore watching their currency lose value and their job opportunities shrink — the foreign optics of Field Marshal Munir's Versailles signatures and Bürgenstock photo-opportunities mean substantively nothing.

For India, the strategic assessment is straightforward. The Pakistan that Munir wants the world to see in the headlines from Lake Lucerne is not the Pakistan that exists on the ground. The Pakistan that exists on the ground is a country struggling to maintain coherent state authority across substantial portions of its own territory, while its Field Marshal collects mediator credentials abroad. The contradiction will not resolve itself through more diplomatic photo-opportunities. It can only be addressed through what Pakistan has consistently refused to undertake — substantive political accommodation of legitimate domestic demands, abandonment of the proxy-warfare doctrine, comprehensive reform of the institutional pathologies that produced the current crisis, and re-establishment of the basic state-citizen contract that decades of military-establishment dominance have progressively eroded.

For now, on the morning of 23 June 2026, what stands on the diplomatic record is this: Pakistan's Field Marshal mediated a Memorandum at Versailles, presided over a quadrilateral at Bürgenstock, and basked in White House praise. Pakistan's own province of Balochistan recorded 254 attacks last year. Pakistan's war with Afghanistan grinds into its ninth month. Pakistan sits atop the 2026 Global Terrorism Index. The contradiction between the optics and the substance has become the central fact of Pakistan's current strategic moment. It is, for India and for the wider region, the most important strategic reality to understand correctly.
