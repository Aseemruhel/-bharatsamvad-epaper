LEAD: Pakistan-occupied Jammu and Kashmir (POK) has entered its most serious civil-unrest moment in years, with at least 11 people killed in clashes between police and protesters in Rawalakot on Sunday, 7 June 2026 — four days after the POK Home Department's proscription of the Joint Awami Action Committee (JAAC) under the Anti-Terrorism Act, 2014. The Poonch District Commissioner Sardar Waheed Khan confirmed to Pakistani daily Dawn that seven civilians were killed in the clash; the POK Inspector General of Police Liaqat Ali Malik confirmed that four police personnel also died in the same incident, with 23 officers injured and at least 40 civilians wounded.

The escalation comes at a politically charged moment. The POK Supreme Court, in an advisory opinion issued on a reference filed by POK President Chaudhry Latif Akbar, has ruled over the weekend that the 12 legislative seats reserved for Kashmiri refugees living in Pakistan — the abolition of which had become JAAC's principal current demand — are constitutionally protected and cannot be removed without a formal constitutional amendment. The ruling lands days before the POK general elections scheduled for 27 July 2026.

## What happened in Rawalakot

The Rawalakot violence is reported to have been triggered by an earlier incident. According to the chronology pieced together from Dawn, Al Jazeera, AFP and other Pakistani reporting, a Rawalakot trader was shot during a confrontation with security personnel on the night of Friday, 5 June. His body was moved to the Combined Military Hospital (CMH) mortuary. Plans to hold his funeral on Saturday were postponed, and the body was returned to the hospital pending post-mortem.

On Sunday, JAAC supporters gathered outside the CMH in connection with the trader's death. The gathering escalated into a confrontation with police and other security personnel. According to the police version subsequently issued in Muzaffarabad, "armed supporters" of JAAC opened fire on security forces and surrounded the hospital, disrupting medical services and damaging public and private property. JAAC has rejected this characterisation and described what happened as a state-led action against unarmed protesters.

JAAC leader Shaukat Nawaz Mir, in a video statement posted on X after the clash, said: "The state has begun a massacre of our people in Rawalakot." He pledged that the alliance would proceed with the long march planned for 9 June.

## The Supreme Court ruling

The advisory opinion of the POK Supreme Court, issued on a reference sent by POK President Chaudhry Latif Akbar, addresses what has become the principal political demand of the JAAC's current mobilisation: the abolition of 12 reserved seats in the POK Legislative Assembly. These seats are designated for Kashmiri refugees who migrated to Pakistan-administered areas after the 1947 partition and Pakistan's accession claim to the territory.

JAAC's argument has been that these refugee seats — voted on through procedures outside Muzaffarabad — function as a mechanism through which mainstream Pakistani political parties influence the formation of governments in POK, diluting the political weight of the territory's resident population. The presidential reference had sought the Court's opinion on five questions, including whether the POK Legislative Assembly was constitutionally competent to amend its own constitutional framework to alter these reserved seats.

The Court has ruled that the 12 reserved seats are part of POK's constitutional architecture and cannot be abolished without a formal constitutional amendment — a high procedural threshold. The Court observed, in its advisory opinion, that any amendment in the region's constitution was "not a concession to be wrested" from the government.

The ruling effectively shuts down, at the judicial level, the immediate avenue through which JAAC had sought to achieve its central demand. For the alliance, the timing — days before the planned long march and weeks before the 27 July elections — concentrates the political moment further.

## The crackdown so far

The 72 hours since the JAAC's proscription on 5 June have included a sequence of measures consistent with a preventive security posture. Mobile data and internet services have been suspended across Muzaffarabad, Rawalakot, Poonch, Mirpur and the Neelum Valley, restricting the flow of independent information from the territory. The JAAC central office in Muzaffarabad was sealed by authorities on Sunday. A formal ban on large gatherings was imposed in Muzaffarabad on the same day.

Arrests have continued across multiple rounds. Following the Sunday clash in Rawalakot, the Poonch Commissioner and POK IG confirmed that 30 people had been taken into custody late on Sunday; this is in addition to scores of JAAC leaders and activists reportedly arrested in the days immediately following the proscription.

The POK administration has issued a public travel advisory urging both domestic and foreign visitors to postpone trips to the territory until 20 June. University examinations originally scheduled for early June have been postponed.

In Muzaffarabad on Monday, markets were operational and law enforcement personnel patrolled the city, with residents reportedly having rushed to shops over the weekend to stock up ahead of expected protests and lockdowns.

## The long march

JAAC's stated plan for 9 June and the days following is a long march beginning in Bhimber, moving through Mirpur, Kotli and Poonch, and culminating in Muzaffarabad on 10 June for a sit-in outside the POK Legislative Assembly. Despite the proscription, the deaths, the sealed offices and the communication restrictions, JAAC has publicly stated that the march will proceed.

POK officials have publicly stated they expect a "limited response" to the strike call given the recent crackdown, and have indicated that any large gathering will be treated as an unlawful assembly under the Anti-Terrorism Act, 2014.

The interaction between these two positions over the next 48 hours — JAAC's stated commitment to the march, and the government's stated commitment to prevent it — will determine the immediate trajectory of the situation.

## International and rights-monitoring response

International press coverage of the situation has expanded over the past 72 hours, with reporting from Al Jazeera, AFP, Reuters, Dawn and other outlets covering Sunday's clashes and the broader crackdown. The communications blackout, however, has significantly constrained the flow of independent information from the territory itself.

Human-rights organisations and Kashmiri diaspora groups in the United Kingdom, the United States and other countries have organised demonstrations highlighting the situation. Independent verification of casualty claims beyond the confirmed 11 has been difficult under the current communications environment, and rights advocates have called for independent assessments of conditions on the ground.

Some activists and diaspora commentators have drawn comparisons between the current PoK situation and the political-suppression dynamics of 1971 in former East Pakistan. Such comparisons are politically charged and historically contested. What is more defensibly stated is that the use of anti-terrorism legislation against a civic alliance focused on economic and constitutional demands — combined with sustained communication blackouts, mass arrests, and the use of lethal force during civilian protests — has prompted significant international concern.

## What the JAAC mobilisation has been about

It is worth restating, in the middle of an escalating security situation, what JAAC was originally mobilised around. The alliance emerged in 2023 as a civic platform organised primarily around three concrete grievances: high electricity tariffs in a region that itself produces significant hydropower, the withdrawal of wheat subsidies, and broader questions about the distribution of hydropower revenues between federal authorities, the POK government, and the local population.

These are not, on their face, security-coded grievances. They are economic and governance grievances that JAAC translated into mass civic mobilisation. The October 2025 Muzaffarabad Agreement between the POK government, federal authorities and JAAC committed to a 38-point reform agenda including subsidised flour, electricity tariff relief, hydropower-revenue arrangements, and the abolition of the 12 refugee seats. The 90-day implementation deadline for that agreement lapsed in January 2026 without full delivery, according to the alliance — a gap that JAAC has cited as the reason for its renewed mobilisation in May and June 2026.

## Why this matters

The current escalation has implications well beyond the immediate situation. Within Pakistan, it raises questions about the application of anti-terrorism legislation to civic movements organised around economic grievances, the use of communications blackouts as a routine response to protests, and the relationship between federal and POK authorities on questions of local governance.

For POK itself, the situation places considerable pressure on the credibility of the 27 July election — the participation rates, the freedom of campaigning, and the political environment in which voting will occur are all now open questions that depend on what happens in the next several weeks.

For international observers of South Asian human rights and governance, the current episode constitutes one of the more significant moments of civic unrest in POK in recent memory. How the situation is resolved — through political accommodation, judicial intervention, sustained security action, or some combination — will be a meaningful data point in the broader assessment of the trajectory of civic space in Pakistan and its administered territories.

## The road ahead

The most immediate question is what happens during the 9-10 June long march. The Pakistani administration's preferred outcome is a low-turnout strike that fails to materialise as a sustained mobilisation. JAAC's stated objective is a successful march that demonstrates continued political resonance despite the proscription and the crackdown. The reality on the ground over the next 48 hours will be the first test of which of these proves more accurate.

Beyond the immediate horizon, four questions will define the trajectory. Whether the 27 July election proceeds on schedule and with credible participation. Whether the substantive economic and constitutional grievances at the heart of JAAC's mobilisation receive any meaningful engagement from the POK and federal governments — particularly given the Supreme Court's narrowing of the constitutional pathway on refugee seats. Whether the proscription stands as a long-term legal instrument or is moderated as a political accommodation is sought. And whether the communications blackout is lifted in a way that allows independent monitoring of the situation to resume.

For the residents of Muzaffarabad, Rawalakot, Bhimber, Mirpur, Poonch and the Neelum Valley, what 9 June 2026 looks like will be the most immediate test. The wider story — of how a civic movement organised around electricity bills and constitutional representation has become a major civil-unrest situation under counter-terrorism law — is one that the coming weeks will continue to write.
