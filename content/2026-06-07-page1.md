LEAD: The government of Pakistan-occupied Jammu and Kashmir (AJK) on 5 June 2026 declared the Jammu Kashmir Joint Awami Action Committee (JK-JAAC) a proscribed organisation under the Anti-Terrorism Act 2014, placing the civil rights alliance — along with its associated names and entities — on the First Schedule of the regional anti-terrorism law. The notification, issued by the AJK Home Department late Friday night with presidential approval, came just three days before the alliance's planned territory-wide shutdown and "long march" on 9 June, and on the same day the AJK Election Commission announced general elections for 27 July 2026.

The ban has been followed by a wide-ranging police crackdown in which independent reports cite the arrest of approximately 72 individuals linked to the organisation, the imposition of an internet and mobile data shutdown in several districts, and a request by the AJK Inspector General of Police for the deployment of 14,000 additional federal paramilitary personnel between 7 and 21 June. The University of AJK on Friday postponed its Spring 2026 examinations, originally scheduled to commence on 8 June.

## What the notification does

According to the official notification issued by the AJK Home Department, JK-JAAC has been added to the First Schedule of the Anti-Terrorism Act 2014, invoking Section 12 of the legislation. The notification covers all alternative names of the organisation — including "Jammu Kashmir Action Committee" and "Joint Awami Action Committee" — and all associated entities.

The Home Department's stated grounds, reported by Pakistani daily Dawn and other Pakistani media outlets, are that the organisation was "engaged in terrorism" and had acted "in a manner prejudicial to peace and security" of the state, with allegations of "creating anarchy", "intimidating the public", "promoting hatred", and "fostering a sense of insecurity within society". The legal effect of the notification is to criminalise any form of support, association, or activity linked to the group, with all attendant restrictions applicable to proscribed organisations.

## The trigger: a long march, and an agreement that did not hold

The immediate context of the ban is the JK-JAAC's planned long march on 9 June. The alliance had announced the territory-wide shutdown and wheel-jam strike weeks earlier, with the stated objective of pressing the AJK and federal governments on what its leadership has described as unimplemented elements of the **Muzaffarabad Agreement** — the accord signed on 4 October 2025 between the AJK government, federal authorities, and JK-JAAC representatives at the close of a week-long civil shutdown last year.

That earlier shutdown, in September-October 2025, had paralysed AJK for several days, was met with an internet suspension by authorities, and ended with the signing of a written agreement that included commitments on electricity tariffs, wheat subsidies, hydropower revenue distribution to the region, compensation for affected families, and formal inquiries into incidents of violence during the protests. The agreement carried a 90-day implementation deadline, which expired in January 2026 without full delivery, according to the alliance.

JK-JAAC's renewed mobilisation in May and June has, by the alliance's own framing, been a response to that implementation gap. The 9 June long march was announced in that context, and the alliance has rejected official suggestions that the timing is connected to the AJK election cycle — pointing out that its protest date was made public before the Election Commission announced the 27 July poll date.

## The chronology

A reconstruction of the timeline, based on Pakistani media reporting:

May 2024 saw the first major Awami Action Committee mobilisations in AJK, sparked by electricity tariff increases and the withdrawal of wheat subsidies. September-October 2025 brought a week-long shutdown and the Muzaffarabad Agreement on 4 October. January 2026 marked the lapse of the 90-day implementation deadline. On 30 May 2026, government proposals on the refugee-seats question were rejected at an All Parties Conference that JK-JAAC chose to boycott. Between 5 and 6 June, the government issued a travel advisory, formally banned the alliance, began the police crackdown and reportedly suspended mobile internet services across parts of the territory. The Election Commission's announcement of 27 July elections came on the same day as the ban.

The compression of these events into a 48-hour window — the formal ban, the arrests, the internet suspension, the paramilitary deployment request, the election schedule announcement, the postponement of university exams — is itself a notable feature of the current moment.

## The economic backdrop

What JK-JAAC began as, and what it has consistently said it is, is an alliance of civic and trade groups organised around three interlocking grievances. Electricity tariffs in PoJK — despite the region producing significant hydropower — have long been a public flashpoint, with residents arguing that local generation should translate into more affordable retail rates. The withdrawal of wheat subsidies hit lower-income households directly. And the broader question of how revenues from hydropower projects in the region are distributed — between the federal government, the AJK government, and the local population — has remained a recurring source of contention.

These are not, on their face, security-coded issues. Most observers — including independent journalists and rights monitors who have reported from AJK over the past two years — have characterised JK-JAAC's mobilisation as a *civic and economic* movement rather than a separatist or armed one. The Pakistani government's choice to designate the alliance under anti-terrorism legislation represents, by any reading, a significant escalation in the framing of what the movement is.

## What the protest is now about

By the alliance's own current articulation, its core political demand has evolved. JK-JAAC is now pushing for the abolition of 12 reserved seats in the AJK Legislative Assembly that are allocated to refugees from Indian-administered Jammu and Kashmir who settled in Pakistan after 1947. The alliance argues that these seats are used by mainstream Pakistani political parties to influence the formation of governments in Muzaffarabad — diluting, in its framing, the political weight of AJK's resident population.

The AJK Legislative Assembly passed a resolution earlier this month retaining the 12 refugee seats and the All Parties Conference of 30 May rejected proposals to abolish them. Pakistan's federal Defence Minister Khawaja Muhammad Asif publicly defended the seats, calling them a "historical and constitutional reality" and characterising the demand for their removal as an attempt to undermine the electoral process. The alliance, in turn, has framed this as an example of how decisions about AJK's representation are being made in Islamabad and Lahore rather than in Muzaffarabad.

## The crackdown

The legal proscription has been accompanied by an immediate operational response on the ground. Independent reporting cites the arrest of approximately 72 individuals in the first 24 hours after the notification, with the AJK Police saying it had recovered alleged weapons and documents. A pro-government source places the figure at 35-40 "key individuals", while the alliance has rejected the underlying allegations.

The internet shutdown — which had also been imposed during the September-October 2025 strike — was reportedly in place across parts of the territory from late 4 June and is expected to remain until 12 June. Mobile data and internet-based calling services have been affected, with prior reporting from such episodes documenting significant disruption to academic activities, online businesses, freelance work, and routine communication. Convoys of security personnel were seen entering Muzaffarabad on the day of the ban.

The 14,000 additional federal paramilitary personnel requested by the AJK Inspector General of Police for the 7-21 June window is, by Pakistani policing standards, a substantial deployment for a sub-national administrative unit of AJK's size.

## How analysts and rights monitors are reading it

Pakistani columnists and editorials in independent outlets — including reporting in *Dawn*, *Pakistan Today*, and *Voice PK* — have noted the preventive character of the ban (issued *before* the planned protest rather than in response to events) and the timing relative to the election schedule announcement. The Pakistani daily *Daily Pakistan* explicitly framed the question in editorial terms: *"What's behind the ban on Jammu Kashmir Joint Awami Action Committee?"* — a framing that itself reflects a degree of scrutiny within Pakistan of the government's choice.

Internationally, rights organisations including Access Now's Keep It On coalition have, in the broader context of South Asian internet shutdowns, repeatedly criticised the practice of suspending digital services as a routine policing response to civic protests. The use of anti-terrorism legislation against civic movements — distinct from organisations engaged in armed activity — has been the subject of recurring concern in reporting by Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, and the International Commission of Jurists on Pakistan's broader application of the Anti-Terrorism Act.

Indian strategic affairs analysts have, in published commentary over the past two years, drawn attention to the widening gap between the formal language of "Azad" Kashmir and the lived governance experience in the territory. The pattern of unresolved economic grievances giving rise to mass civic mobilisations — and the state's response moving rapidly from negotiation to proscription — is being read in New Delhi as both a continuation of a long-standing structural problem in the territory's governance and a particularly visible recent escalation.

## The marginalisation question

What appears, viewed against the documentary record, to be at the centre of this story is not the question of whether a particular civic alliance has crossed legal lines. It is the question of how a region whose population has been mobilising over electricity bills, wheat subsidies, hydropower revenue shares and constitutional representation finds itself, three years into a public campaign on these issues, formally classified under a counter-terrorism framework — even as the agreement that the same government had signed only eight months earlier remains, on the alliance's account, largely unimplemented.

Independent observers who have covered the AJK situation over the past several years have consistently noted that the population's grievances are concrete, locally articulated, and concerned with daily-life issues rather than separatist or external agendas. The use of anti-terrorism legislation to manage what is, by any reasonable description, a civic-economic mobilisation reflects a choice about the nature of the state's relationship with its population — a choice that is the subject of considerable comment in Pakistani journalism and policy commentary.

## The road ahead

The most immediate question is what happens on 9 June. JK-JAAC's leadership has publicly stated that the long march will proceed as planned, ban or no ban; the AJK government has declared that any such gathering will be treated as an illegal assembly under the ATA. The interaction between these two positions over the coming days will determine, in significant part, how the situation evolves.

Beyond 9 June, three open questions matter. The first is whether the 27 July election proceeds on schedule, in what conditions, and with what proportion of public participation. The second is whether the underlying economic and constitutional grievances — electricity, subsidies, hydropower revenue distribution, the refugee seats question — receive any substantive engagement from the AJK and federal governments. And the third is whether the proscription stands as a long-term legal instrument or is moderated as a political accommodation is sought.

For Pakistan's federal authorities, the way this episode is handled has implications well beyond AJK. It speaks to questions about the state's approach to civic dissent more broadly, the durability of negotiated agreements with public movements, and the way Pakistan's institutional architecture engages with regions that sit, constitutionally and politically, in liminal status.

For the residents of AJK, the situation as it stands on 7 June 2026 is one in which a civic alliance organised around concrete economic grievances has been designated under counter-terrorism law, dozens of its members are in custody, internet access is restricted, paramilitary deployment is being substantially increased, and an electoral process has been announced under those conditions. How that combination plays out over the coming weeks will be watched closely — by the population of AJK itself, by Pakistani journalists and rights monitors, by Indian strategic-affairs analysts, and by international observers of the broader South Asian human-rights and governance landscape.
