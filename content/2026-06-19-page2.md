LEAD: India has delivered one of the sharpest diplomatic rebuttals to Pakistan in recent UN history, with First Secretary Anupama Singh — exercising India's right of reply at the 62nd Session of the UN Human Rights Council in Geneva on Wednesday, 18 June 2026 — calling Pakistan "a living example of a Frankenstein state, which is shocked when its own monster bites back", rejecting Pakistan and Organisation of Islamic Cooperation (OIC) references to Jammu and Kashmir, and declaring the 1960 Indus Waters Treaty "outdated" and unsuited to the realities of the present.

The statement, made during the Interactive Dialogue on the UN High Commissioner's annual report, marked a noticeably escalated register in India's diplomatic responses to Pakistan-OIC interventions at international human rights forums. It also formalised, at the UN level, the analytical position India has been developing on the Indus Waters Treaty since the treaty's suspension following the Pahalgam terror attack of April 2025.

## What was said, and why it matters

The remarks were triggered by references to Jammu and Kashmir made by Pakistan and the OIC during the dialogue session. Ms Singh's response was unequivocal and structured. "India is compelled to exercise this right of reply in response to references made to it by Pakistan and the OIC. We categorically reject the baseless and malicious allegations made by Pakistan," she said.

On Jammu and Kashmir, Ms Singh reiterated India's long-standing position with particular sharpness: "Jammu and Kashmir was, is, and will always remain an integral and inalienable part of India. The only unresolved issue is Pakistan's illegal occupation of Indian territories and their return."

The diplomat then made the framing that has dominated international coverage of the statement. "This is the country whose sitting Defence Minister boasts of hosting, training, and deploying terrorists as state policy, and yet Pakistan calls itself a victim of terrorism. Indeed, a paradox which only Pakistan could sustain. It is a living example of a Frankenstein state, which is shocked when its own monster bites back."

The reference to Pakistan's "sitting Defence Minister" was unmistakably to Khawaja Asif, who has on multiple occasions in recent years acknowledged — in interviews with international outlets — that Pakistan has had a history of supporting and using armed proxies as instruments of state policy. The "monster bites back" framing addresses the well-documented pattern in which armed groups originally nurtured by the Pakistani security establishment have, in successive periods, turned their operations against the Pakistani state itself.

## The Indus Waters Treaty: from technical arrangement to political issue

A substantial portion of Ms Singh's statement was dedicated to the Indus Waters Treaty — and represented, in policy terms, the most analytically significant element of the intervention.

"Our position on the Indus Water Treaty is well known. It defies logic that a state which exports terror as an instrument of policy continues to demand the privileges of cooperation predicated on goodwill and friendship," Ms Singh said.

She continued: "It is equally undeniable that the treaty is now outdated. No technical arrangement can remain frozen in time while the world around it is transformed."

A treaty negotiated in 1960, the diplomat argued, "cannot be treated as a perpetual entitlement insulated from accountability, detached from present-day realities and untouched by the profound changes of the past six decades".

This is a substantive analytical evolution in India's official framing of the Indus Waters Treaty. Where earlier Indian statements focused on the treaty being in abeyance pending Pakistani action against terror infrastructure — implicitly leaving the door open to a future return to the treaty's operation — the language of the UNHRC statement frames the treaty itself as unsuited to the present, suggesting that any future cooperation will need a fundamentally new framework, not a return to the 1960 arrangement.

## Pakistan-Occupied Jammu and Kashmir: human rights as accountability

Ms Singh also referenced, with notable directness, the human rights situation in Pakistan-occupied Jammu and Kashmir — addressing the recent pattern of state crackdowns on protest movements in the region.

"Denial of basic freedoms has brought matters to a point where even demands for bread, electricity, rights, and dignity are met with bullets and brutality," she said. The reference connects to the documented violence, including in Rawalakot, against the Joint Awami Action Committee long march and related civil-rights mobilisations through May and early June 2026.

The deployment of the human rights framing — placing Pakistan's conduct in its own occupied territory at the UNHRC rather than allowing the council's agenda to be defined by Pakistan-OIC complaints about Indian Kashmir — is one of the substantive achievements of the statement. It transforms the strategic geometry of the Kashmir conversation at the UN, putting the spotlight on the human rights situation in Pakistan-administered territories at exactly the forum where Pakistan and the OIC have, for decades, sought to highlight allegations against India.

## The wider strategic context

The statement lands within a wider pattern of escalating Indian diplomatic firmness on Pakistan, visible across multiple UN forums over the past year. India's Permanent Representative to the UN, Ambassador P Harish, made comparable statements at the UN Security Council in January 2026 (on Operation Sindoor) and in June 2025 (during the UNSC debate on Children and Armed Conflict). Ms Singh herself delivered a sharp statement at the 61st UNHRC Session in February 2026, in which she called J&K's development trajectory "more than double the recent bailout package Pakistan sought from the IMF" and observed that "it is hard to take lectures on democracy from a country where civilian governments rarely complete their terms."

The June 2026 statement extends this register substantially. The "Frankenstein state" framing — vivid, quotable and analytically pointed — places Pakistan's state behaviour in a category that previous statements had described only descriptively. The "outdated" framing of the Indus Waters Treaty signals what may be the substantive Indian negotiating position for any future water-sharing conversation: that the 1960 arrangement is not a baseline to return to but a framework that has been overtaken by events and conduct.

## The treaty question and the future

The Indus Waters Treaty has, for sixty-five years, been one of the most consistently cited examples of bilateral cooperation between India and Pakistan despite the broader hostilities of the relationship. Even during the wars of 1965, 1971, and 1999, and through the entire period of the cross-border terrorism that has shaped the relationship since the late 1980s, the treaty held. Its suspension in April 2025, following the Pahalgam attack, was therefore one of the most consequential diplomatic actions India had taken against Pakistan in decades.

Ms Singh's statement at Geneva moves the policy framework one further step. It is one thing for a treaty to be suspended pending action on terrorism — that frames the treaty itself as a tool of pressure. It is another thing to argue that the treaty is "outdated", "frozen in time while the world around it is transformed", and that a 1960 negotiation "cannot be treated as a perpetual entitlement". This framing implies that even a future Pakistani action on terror infrastructure may not, by itself, restore the treaty's operation in its existing form. Any future arrangement, on the logic of Ms Singh's statement, would need to be a new arrangement responsive to present-day realities — not a return to the 1960 framework.

This is a substantive shift, and one that international observers of South Asian water politics will be tracking carefully in the months ahead.

## What it signifies

For India, the UN statement is more than rhetorical flourish. It accomplishes three distinct strategic ends. First, it definitively rejects, on the record, the Pakistan-OIC attempt to internationalise the Kashmir question through UN human rights forums — using the human rights framework against the position of the very actors who deployed it. Second, it positions Pakistan's own conduct, particularly the documented terror sponsorship and the recent crackdowns in PoJK, as the legitimate subject of human rights concern, inverting the rhetorical move that Pakistan and the OIC had attempted. Third, it formally articulates a substantively new Indian position on the Indus Waters Treaty — moving from "suspended pending action" to "fundamentally outdated".

The "Frankenstein state" framing, in this analytical structure, is the rhetorical device that ties the three strands together — combining the terror sponsorship, the internal repression, and the bad-faith demand for cooperation under a treaty framework into a single coherent characterisation. It is the kind of phrase designed to enter the international diplomatic vocabulary, and it has visibly succeeded in doing so within hours of the statement being delivered.

## The road ahead

The UNHRC 62nd Session continues through the coming weeks, and further Indian and Pakistani interventions on the substantive country agenda are expected. The Indus Waters Treaty negotiations — or, as Ms Singh's framing implies, the absence of negotiations toward restoration of the 1960 arrangement — will continue to shape one of the most consequential strands of the India-Pakistan relationship. The wider pattern of Indian diplomatic firmness on Pakistan, visible across UN forums through 2025 and into 2026, appears to be entering an escalated phase rather than tapering off.

For now, what stands on the diplomatic record is this: India has used one of the world's most consequential human rights forums to characterise Pakistan as a Frankenstein state whose conduct has rendered a sixty-five-year-old treaty arrangement unsuited to the present, and has done so in language that will be quoted in international coverage of the India-Pakistan relationship for years to come. The intervention is, by any measure, one of the most pointed Indian diplomatic statements on Pakistan in the history of India's engagement with the UN human rights architecture. The "seasonal theatrics" of Pakistani interventions at the council, as Ms Singh put it, have today met an Indian response calibrated to ensure that the theatrics do not pass without substantive consequence.
