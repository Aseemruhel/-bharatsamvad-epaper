LEAD: India is moving with substantial decisiveness on what has emerged as the most strategically consequential category of raw materials of the present decade — critical minerals. The Government is finalising a ₹2,000 crore Production-Linked Incentive (PLI) scheme for domestic lithium processing and nickel refining, offering a 15 per cent capital subsidy on eligible projects, duty waivers on imported capital goods, and a five-year incentive window beginning April 2026. The scheme is designed to catalyse, at scale, a category of industrial capability that India has historically not possessed — the conversion of raw lithium and nickel ores and concentrates into the battery-grade refined materials that power electric vehicles, grid-scale energy storage, electronics, and the broader clean-energy transition.

The processing scheme adds a critical downstream pillar to the broader ₹34,300 crore National Critical Mineral Mission (NCMM), approved by the Union Cabinet in January 2025 — which now stands as one of the most consequential industrial-strategy initiatives of the current decade. With ₹16,300 crore in direct government expenditure and ₹18,000 crore in expected investment by public sector undertakings spread over a seven-year programme period, the NCMM addresses every stage of the critical-minerals value chain: exploration, mining, beneficiation, processing, recovery from end-of-life products, overseas asset acquisition, and a domestic stockpile programme. Combined with the parallel ₹1,500 crore Critical Mineral Recycling Incentive Scheme, the targeted overseas mining-asset acquisitions led by Khanij Bidesh India Limited (KABIL), and the expanded Geological Survey of India (GSI) exploration programme, India is building — for the first time in its history — a comprehensive national architecture aimed squarely at securing the most strategically consequential raw materials of the present century.

## The processing-incentive scheme: what is being offered

The new scheme, currently in the final stages of inter-ministerial consultation, is calibrated to address the specific economic and technical challenges of establishing lithium and nickel refining capacity in India. The instruments include a 15 per cent capital subsidy on eligible projects — substantially de-risking the upfront capital expenditure that any new processing facility requires. Duty waivers on imported capital goods reduce the cost of acquiring the specialised equipment (refining furnaces, hydrometallurgical processing systems, solvent-extraction infrastructure, electrolysis units) that domestic suppliers are not yet able to provide at scale. Production-linked incentives — disbursed on the basis of demonstrated output and sales — ensure that the support flows specifically to projects that actually deliver refined material into the Indian and global supply chain.

The five-year incentive window aligns with the typical period required to establish a new processing facility from financial close through commissioning to stable commercial operations. Incentive caps based on annual sales ensure that the support is proportionate to the operational scale of the facility, while the per-project cap structure prevents excessive concentration of benefits in a small number of large players. The scheme design is informed by the lessons of India's earlier industrial-scheme deployments — particularly the Production-Linked Incentive frameworks for semiconductors, mobile phones, and solar manufacturing — that have, with progressive refinement, become the dominant policy instrument for catalysing strategic-sector capabilities.

## The strategic logic: why this matters

The case for the scheme rests on a structural reality of the global battery supply chain. India's battery demand is projected to rise from approximately 28 gigawatt-hours (GWh) in 2025 to approximately 210 GWh by 2030 — a more than seven-fold increase in just five years. By the mid-2040s, the India Energy Storage Alliance projects demand of more than 700 GWh annually. This demand will be driven by electric vehicles (which currently account for approximately 60 per cent of demand), grid-scale energy storage (approximately 40 per cent), and the steadily growing demand from consumer electronics, industrial applications, and defence systems.

Producing battery cells at this scale requires refined critical minerals — lithium carbonate, lithium hydroxide, nickel sulphate, cobalt sulphate, and the corresponding rare earth and graphite materials. The substantive challenge is that, globally, the refining of these critical minerals is heavily concentrated in a small number of countries. China refines approximately 74 per cent of global lithium, approximately 35 per cent of nickel, and approximately 80 per cent of cobalt. China also controls approximately 98 per cent of global lithium-iron-phosphate (LFP) cathode active material production. India's current lithium refining capacity is, by independent assessment, effectively zero. Every gigawatt-hour of battery cell that is, will be, or could be manufactured in India therefore depends — at the most upstream and strategically consequential point in the value chain — on refining operations that India does not currently possess.

The ₹2,000 crore processing-incentive scheme is, in policy terms, the deliberate strategic answer to this. Rather than continuing to import refined materials and concentrate India's industrial efforts further downstream — battery cell manufacturing, pack assembly, and EV integration — the new scheme moves a substantial portion of the value chain upstream, into India. Domestic refining capacity means strategic autonomy at the most critical point of the supply chain. It means reduced exposure to the geopolitical risks associated with concentration of refining in a single country. It means substantive Indian value-addition in a category where India has, until now, been largely a price-taker. And it means catalysing an entirely new industrial sector whose growth trajectory tracks the country's electric-vehicle and clean-energy transitions.

## The National Critical Mineral Mission: the architecture

The ₹2,000 crore processing scheme sits within the broader architecture of the National Critical Mineral Mission. The Mission's principal strategic objectives, set out in the January 2025 Cabinet approval and progressively elaborated in subsequent policy notifications, are to build India's self-reliance across the critical-minerals value chain; to ensure secure access to twenty-four notified critical minerals through a combination of domestic production, overseas acquisition, recycling, and strategic stockpiling; and to position India as a credible alternative node in the global critical-minerals supply chain.

The Mission encompasses six functional pillars. **Exploration**: an ambitious target of 1,200 domestic exploration projects by 2030-31, with a focus on identifying viable reserves of at least 15 priority critical minerals including graphite, lithium, potash, and rare earth elements. **Mining and Auction**: a fast-track regulatory approval process for critical mineral mining projects, supported by the auction of mineral blocks across multiple tranches. **Processing**: the ₹2,000 crore PLI scheme announced today, plus capacity-building infrastructure including dedicated mineral-processing parks. **Recovery and Recycling**: the ₹1,500 crore Critical Mineral Recycling Incentive Scheme targeting recovery of 400 kilotonnes of secondary materials. **Overseas Acquisition**: targeted acquisition by Indian PSUs and private-sector partners of 50 mining assets worldwide. **Research and Talent**: a Centre of Excellence on Critical Minerals supporting research, technology development, and skill formation.

The Mines and Minerals (Development and Regulation) Amendment Act, 2023 — the legislative foundation for much of the Mission's operational framework — formally notified twenty-four critical and strategic minerals under Part D of Schedule I. These are the minerals India has identified as essential for its clean-energy transition, defence capabilities, electronics manufacturing, and broader industrial security. For most of these, India has either negligible domestic reserves or production that lags substantially behind demand — resulting in import dependence that, in many cases, exceeds 80 per cent and contributes to an annual foreign-exchange outflow of more than ₹80,000 crore.

## What India has already achieved

The Mission's effective rollout has produced concrete deliverables already visible on the ground. The Geological Survey of India has substantially scaled up its critical-minerals exploration programme — from 59 exploration projects in the 2021-22 field season to 227 projects in 2025-26, a nearly four-fold expansion in just four years. The Ministry of Mines has notified 55 unique mineral blocks for auction across five tranches; of these, 34 have been successfully auctioned, putting substantial domestic mining potential into productive industrial use.

Khanij Bidesh India Limited (KABIL) — the joint-venture vehicle created specifically for overseas critical-mineral acquisitions, with participation from National Aluminium Company (NALCO), Hindustan Copper Limited (HCL), and Mineral Exploration Corporation Limited (MECL) — has acquired a lithium block in Argentina, securing one of India's first major overseas footprints in the lithium triangle that holds approximately 60 per cent of the world's identified lithium resources. Indian entities have secured exploration rights over approximately 9,000 square kilometres of mineral-rich territory in Zambia, opening access to copper, cobalt, and other critical minerals essential to the country's battery and electronics manufacturing ambitions.

The Critical Mineral Recycling Incentive Scheme, with an outlay of ₹1,500 crore over six years (FY 2025-26 to FY 2030-31), opened its application window in October 2025 and is targeting the recovery of 400 kilotonnes of critical minerals from secondary sources — including e-waste, spent lithium-ion battery scrap, end-of-life permanent magnets, catalytic converters, and alloy scraps. This "urban mining" pillar substantially complements the primary-mining and overseas-acquisition strategies, providing an additional supply pathway less exposed to geopolitical and price volatility.

## How the processing scheme fits the wider picture

The ₹2,000 crore processing incentive complements rather than duplicates the existing schemes. The Production-Linked Incentive for Advanced Chemistry Cell battery manufacturing — launched in October 2021 with an outlay of approximately ₹18,100 crore — supports the downstream segment of cell manufacturing itself. The proposed new ₹12,000 crore battery-components incentive scheme, reported earlier this month, will support the intermediate segment of cathode active materials, anode active materials, electrolytes, and separators. The ₹2,000 crore processing scheme now opens up the most upstream Indian segment — the refining of raw lithium and nickel into battery-grade materials.

Together, these three policy instruments create — for the first time in India's industrial history — a coherent and well-funded architecture across the entire battery value chain. Mineral exploration and extraction (NCMM and KABIL). Refining and processing (the new ₹2,000 crore scheme). Cell components (the proposed ₹12,000 crore scheme). Cell manufacturing (ACC PLI). Pack assembly and EV integration (multiple state-level and central frameworks). Recycling and circular-economy infrastructure (the ₹1,500 crore Recycling Incentive Scheme).

This is the architecture that has, in other major economies, taken decades to assemble. India's compression of the timeline reflects both the urgency of the strategic challenge — the global race for critical-minerals security is now fully under way — and the maturity of Indian industrial-policy thinking that has emerged from the experience of building the world's third-largest solar manufacturing sector, the country's pharmaceutical-export champion status, and the rapidly-expanding semiconductor and electronics manufacturing base.

## The international context

The Indian initiative arrives at a moment of substantial global activity in the critical-minerals space. The European Union's Critical Raw Materials Act of 2024 set ambitious benchmarks for European Union extraction, processing, and recycling of critical minerals by 2030. The United States has expanded its critical-minerals incentive frameworks through the Inflation Reduction Act and subsequent legislation, alongside expanded mineral-cooperation partnerships with Australia, Canada, and resource-rich partner nations. Australia, Chile, the Democratic Republic of the Congo, and other major mineral-producing countries are progressively negotiating offtake and processing partnerships with consuming nations seeking supply security.

India's emerging architecture positions the country as a credible partner in this global re-architecting. The combination of substantial domestic demand (the 210 GWh projection by 2030 makes India one of the most consequential battery markets globally), substantive incentive frameworks (now exceeding ₹50,000 crore across the integrated battery and minerals architecture), expanding overseas acquisition activity (KABIL Argentina, Zambia exploration, ongoing discussions in Australia, Chile, and across Africa), and a maturing domestic regulatory framework makes India one of the more attractive destinations for international critical-minerals capital looking for non-China processing alternatives.

The Quad Critical Minerals Initiative — the cooperation framework among India, Japan, Australia, and the United States, which has progressively expanded since the early 2020s — provides one institutional anchor for India's international critical-minerals engagement. Bilateral partnerships with Australia (under the India-Australia Critical Minerals Investment Partnership), with Chile, with several African resource-holders, and with European partners seeking alternative supply lines together create the international layer of India's critical-minerals strategy.

## The clean-energy and defence dimensions

The strategic value of critical minerals extends well beyond batteries. Critical minerals are essential inputs for the entirety of India's clean-energy transition: wind turbines (rare earth elements for permanent magnets), solar panels (silicon, silver, copper), grid infrastructure (copper, aluminium), and hydrogen electrolysers (platinum group metals). For defence systems — radars, communications, propulsion, advanced electronics — the same categories of critical minerals are essential inputs. The Strategic Petroleum Reserve thinking that India institutionalised in the 1990s for oil security finds its parallel today in the Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve thinking embedded within the NCMM's stockpiling pillar.

The defence dimension is particularly consequential given India's substantial defence-manufacturing programme. The ongoing ₹38,424 crore in defence exports for FY 2025-26 (an all-time record); the 145 active defence-exporting companies; the BrahMos, Pinaka, Akash, and other strategic platform programmes — all depend on critical-mineral inputs that, until now, India has been substantially importing. Domestic refining capability in lithium and nickel, alongside the broader NCMM framework, directly strengthens India's defence-industrial autonomy.

## The road ahead

In the immediate horizon, the ₹2,000 crore processing-incentive scheme will move through final inter-ministerial consultation, Cabinet approval, and notification — with applications expected to open shortly after the April 2026 commencement. Industry interest is already substantial: domestic conglomerates including Vedanta, Hindalco, Adani Group, JSW Steel, Reliance Industries, and Tata Group have all signalled interest in critical-minerals processing investments, alongside potential international partners.

In the medium term, the integrated rollout of the NCMM architecture will progressively deliver outcomes across the six functional pillars. Domestic mineral production from auctioned blocks will begin scaling up over the coming years. Overseas acquisitions through KABIL and private-sector partners will expand. The recycling ecosystem will mature. Processing facilities under the new scheme will reach commercial commissioning by 2028-29. Battery component manufacturing under the proposed ₹12,000 crore scheme will fill the intermediate gap.

In the longer term — the horizon to 2030 and beyond — India is positioning itself for a fundamentally different role in the global critical-minerals economy. Instead of being a downstream importer dependent on a small number of refining nodes, India is building toward becoming a substantive participant across the value chain, with domestic refining capacity, overseas mineral access, a sophisticated recycling industry, and strategic stockpile reserves. For a country that imports more than 85 per cent of its crude oil and substantial portions of its natural gas requirement, the case for early and decisive action on critical-minerals security is structurally compelling — and the policy architecture being assembled is genuinely commensurate with the scale of the strategic challenge.

For now, on the morning of 23 June 2026, what stands on the record is this: India is committing ₹2,000 crore specifically to lithium and nickel processing, on top of the ₹34,300 crore National Critical Mineral Mission, on top of the ₹1,500 crore Recycling Incentive Scheme, on top of the proposed ₹12,000 crore battery-components scheme, on top of the ₹18,100 crore Advanced Chemistry Cell PLI — together representing one of the largest integrated industrial-strategy commitments in India's recent history. The Aatmanirbhar Bharat doctrine, applied to the most strategically consequential raw materials of the present century, is now visibly assembling the architecture that future India will inhabit. The work continues; the trajectory is established.
