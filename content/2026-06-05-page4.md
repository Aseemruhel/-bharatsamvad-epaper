LEAD: The University of Liverpool has received formal approval from the Government of India to establish its branch campus in Bengaluru, Karnataka, with classes scheduled to begin in August 2026. The institution, founded in 1881 and a member of the United Kingdom's Russell Group of research-intensive universities, becomes the first Russell Group member with a full physical campus in India — a milestone in the implementation of the National Education Policy 2020.

The Letter of Approval was handed over on Thursday, 4 June 2026, by Dr Vineet Joshi, Secretary of the Department of Higher Education in the Ministry of Education and Chairman of the University Grants Commission, to Professor Richard Grose, Provost of the University of Liverpool Bengaluru. The ceremony was held during the first ministerial review of the India-UK Vision 2035 bilateral framework.

## The setting

The handover was witnessed by Union Education Minister Dharmendra Pradhan, External Affairs Minister Dr S. Jaishankar, and UK Foreign Secretary the Rt. Hon. Yvette Cooper — the latter making her first official visit to India in her current role. Foreign Secretary Vikram Misri and the UK High Commissioner to India Lindy Cameron were also present, signalling the diplomatic weight both governments are attaching to the development.

Alongside the Liverpool approval, a Memorandum of Understanding between King's College London and India's National Maritime Foundation was also exchanged at the same event — bringing two simultaneous India-UK education and research announcements under the Vision 2035 umbrella.

## A year-long journey

The University of Liverpool had received an earlier Letter of Intent on 26 May 2025, in New Delhi, under the UGC (Setting Up and Operation of Campuses of Foreign Higher Educational Institutions in India) Regulations, 2023 — the regulatory framework that operationalised the NEP 2020 provision allowing foreign universities to establish campuses in India. Between the Letter of Intent and the Letter of Approval, the university completed academic planning, infrastructure development and regulatory compliance verification by the UGC.

The Letter of Approval is the final clearance required for the campus to formally commence operations. With it in hand, admissions — which had already opened in November 2025 — can now proceed to enrolment and classroom delivery beginning August 2026, with classes scheduled to start in September.

## What the Bengaluru campus will offer

The University of Liverpool Bengaluru campus will, in its initial phase, offer both undergraduate and postgraduate programmes across a range of disciplines: Computer Science; Data and Artificial Intelligence; Business Management; Finance; Biomedical Sciences; and Game Design. The portfolio is expected to expand in subsequent years.

According to the university, the programmes will largely reflect Liverpool's existing offerings in the United Kingdom, with adaptations to align with Indian industry needs and regulatory context. The academic standards, the institution has stated, will be maintained in line with the UK campus — a particularly significant point in the broader debate over how branch campuses of foreign universities will be benchmarked.

Faculty is expected to follow a hybrid model: a mix of Liverpool-based academics travelling to Bengaluru and locally hired professors who meet the university's standards. A faculty training programme led by the Bengaluru campus Provost is intended to ensure quality consistency across both sides of the institution.

## The Russell Group milestone

The Russell Group is the United Kingdom's grouping of twenty-four research-intensive universities — a cohort that includes Oxford, Cambridge, Imperial College London, University College London, the London School of Economics, the University of Edinburgh, the University of Manchester and others. Membership of the group is widely treated, in higher-education circles, as a marker of research depth, faculty profile and research income.

That a Russell Group university is now establishing a full Indian campus is the most significant signal yet that the NEP 2020 architecture for foreign universities — operationalised through the 2023 UGC Regulations — is succeeding in attracting institutions at the top end of the global higher education hierarchy. Earlier approvals under the same framework have included Deakin University and the University of Wollongong from Australia, and Southampton University from the UK.

## The Vision 2035 backdrop

The Liverpool announcement was anchored within the first ministerial review of the India-UK Vision 2035 — the bilateral framework that succeeded earlier roadmaps for cooperation between the two countries. The Vision 2035 framework spans trade, defence, technology, education, science, and people-to-people ties.

UK Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper's presence at the handover — on her first official visit to India — was a public signal of how seriously London is treating the partnership. Multiple education and research-collaboration announcements being clustered into one ministerial moment, rather than being spread across several discrete announcements, is itself a signal of intent.

## Why this matters for India

The case for foreign university campuses in India rests on three propositions. First, that they reduce the financial and logistical burden on Indian students who would otherwise travel abroad for similar-quality programmes — Indians have for years been one of the largest international student cohorts in the UK, US, Australia, and Canada. Second, that they bring research linkages, faculty networks and industry partnerships into India in a way that pure student mobility does not. Third, that they raise the competitive bar for domestic universities, with potential second-order effects on the broader sector.

The counter-arguments are also real and worth acknowledging: that branch campuses may not replicate the full research-and-faculty depth of the home university, that fees at such campuses may remain out of reach for the majority of Indian students, and that the long-term effect on Indian institutions is yet to be seen. These are legitimate questions that will be answered by how the experiment unfolds rather than by current rhetoric.

## The Bengaluru choice

The University of Liverpool's selection of Bengaluru is consistent with the city's profile as one of India's principal hubs of technology, research, biomedical innovation and higher education. With existing concentrations in computer science and biomedical research, and a substantial corporate and start-up ecosystem in the disciplines that match Liverpool's initial programme portfolio, the city offers an immediate environment for industry partnerships and student placements.

Karnataka's higher-education ecosystem, and Bengaluru's positioning specifically, are likely to benefit from the cluster effect — and other foreign universities considering Indian campuses are reportedly examining Bengaluru, Hyderabad, and Pune among their preferred locations.

## The road ahead

The next stages will be visible: enrolment of the first cohort in August 2026, the operational launch of academic programmes in September, the build-out of faculty hiring and infrastructure, and the gradual expansion of the programme portfolio over subsequent years. The university's stated target of 10,000 students within a decade is, by Indian higher-education standards, a substantial scale of operation.

For the broader Indian policy conversation, the Liverpool approval will be watched as a test case. Its progress over the next several years — both as an academic institution and as a model for international university entry — will shape the trajectory of foreign-university partnerships under NEP 2020 and the 2023 UGC Regulations. Other Russell Group and global research universities are reportedly observing the experience closely.

For now, however, the achievement is real and announceable: India has secured the entry of one of the United Kingdom's elite research universities, on terms that signal a maturing institutional and regulatory architecture for international higher education partnerships.
