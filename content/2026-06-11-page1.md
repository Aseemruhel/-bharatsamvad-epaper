LEAD: President Droupadi Murmu, at the Defence Investiture Ceremony-2026 (Phase I) held at Rashtrapati Bhavan on Monday, 8 June 2026, conferred 51 gallantry awards on personnel of the Indian Armed Forces, the Central Armed Police Forces, and State and Union Territory Police. The ceremony was attended by Vice President C. P. Radhakrishnan, Prime Minister Narendra Modi, and Defence Minister Rajnath Singh — a presence that, in the choreography of such investitures, signals the institutional weight the Government of India places on the citations being read out.

The numbers themselves carry significance: seven Kirti Chakras (two posthumous), fifteen Vir Chakras (three posthumous), and twenty-nine Shaurya Chakras (one posthumous). The fifteen Vir Chakras are the part of this picture that deserves attention first, because in normal years they would not exist. The Vir Chakra is a wartime gallantry award; its appearance in this volume reflects the recognition of valour during Operation Sindoor, India's military exchange with Pakistan in May 2025. The Kirti Chakra and Shaurya Chakra, by contrast, are peacetime honours — the second and third highest, respectively — and the citations across both categories on Monday painted a particular picture of the contemporary Indian operational landscape.

## The seven Kirti Chakras

The Kirti Chakra recipients spanned an unusually wide canvas. Five of the awards went to ground-force personnel — Lance Naik Meenatchi Sundarama of the Rashtriya Rifles, Naib Subedar Doleshwar Subba of the Para (Special Forces), Major Arshdeep Singh of the Assam Rifles, Captain Lalrinawma Sailo of the Para (Special Forces), and, posthumously, Sepoy Janjal Pravin Prabhakar of the Mahar Regiment of the Rashtriya Rifles. A sixth was conferred posthumously on Lieutenant Shashank Tiwari of the Sikkim Scouts. Each of these citations, in the larger pattern of how the Kirti Chakra has been awarded in recent years, sits within the operational geography of counter-insurgency and counter-terrorism in Jammu and Kashmir and the Northeast — the two theatres that have most consistently demanded close-quarter, irregular-warfare courage from Indian Army personnel since the 2000s. The two posthumous citations carry the additional weight such citations always do: a service in which the cost of valour was the cost of life, recognised by the state on behalf of families that will receive both the medal and the long shadow that follows.

The seventh Kirti Chakra is the one that has drawn the most public attention, and for good reason. It was conferred on Prasanth Balakrishnan Nair of the Indian Air Force — fighter pilot, holder of more than three thousand flying hours, and one of the four Indian Gaganyatris selected for India's Gaganyaan crewed spaceflight programme. The award places Nair within a select group of Indian military personnel for whom the line between conventional gallantry and what might be called exploratory courage has begun to blur.

## What the Prasanth Nair award signals

To award a Kirti Chakra — an honour traditionally associated with bravery in armed combat or rescue under fire — to an astronaut-designate is itself a framing choice by the Government of India, and one worth reading carefully. India's space programme has, for decades, been organised around a deliberately civilian, science-led public identity, with the Indian Space Research Organisation presenting itself as a fundamentally civilian agency despite its strategic value. The decision to honour a Gaganyatri through the Kirti Chakra framework alters this register. It treats Nair's preparation for crewed spaceflight as a form of state service of the same moral and institutional character as the kinds of valour traditionally recognised by the award.

This is part of a broader pattern that has been visible across the past eighteen months. Group Captain Shubhanshu Shukla, the first Indian to visit the International Space Station — having travelled there as part of the Axiom-4 mission in mid-2025 — was honoured with the Ashoka Chakra, India's highest peacetime gallantry award, in the Republic Day announcements of January 2026. The decision to use the Ashoka Chakra for a space achievement, rather than a civilian honour such as the Padma Shri or Padma Bhushan, was itself an institutional statement: that frontier-of-knowledge service in the space domain is recognised by the Indian state through its martial honours, not its civilian ones. The Nair Kirti Chakra extends the same logic. The implicit message is that the Indian state now classifies the human exploration of space as a domain of strategic effort that deserves recognition through the framework reserved for valour. Whether one reads this as a militarisation of the space programme or as a long-overdue elevation of the personal courage involved in human spaceflight is a matter of perspective. What is not in dispute is that the framework has shifted, and that the shift was deliberate.

## Navika Sagar Parikrama II and the women of the Indian Navy

The Shaurya Chakra list contains a citation of comparable analytical interest, awarded to Lieutenant Commander Dilna K. and Lieutenant Commander Roopa A. of the Indian Navy for their conduct during Navika Sagar Parikrama II — the Navy's second all-women circumnavigation of the globe, conducted by the same officers as a two-person crew aboard the Indian Naval Sailing Vessel Tarini. Over the course of the expedition, they sailed approximately 25,600 nautical miles across the Indian, Pacific, Atlantic and Southern Oceans, navigating through some of the most demanding maritime environments on earth.

The citation, read out at Rashtrapati Bhavan and posted by the President's Office, identifies two specific elements as the basis of the honour. The first is the navigation of the Drake Passage — the body of water between Cape Horn and the South Shetland Islands, infamous among sailors for the convergence of three oceans, the absence of any sheltering land mass at that latitude, and the consequent capacity to generate storms and seas of a severity rarely encountered elsewhere. The Indian Naval citation records that the vessel heeled severely under storm conditions during this passage, and that recovery of control required exceptional skill and extraordinary courage.

The second element is the achievement of having become the first Indians to reach Point Nemo by sailboat. Point Nemo is the oceanic pole of inaccessibility — the location in the Pacific Ocean that is furthest from any land in any direction, at coordinates approximately 48 degrees south and 123 degrees west. The closest inhabited landmass to Point Nemo is, at any given moment, the International Space Station as it passes overhead. To reach Point Nemo by sailboat is to place oneself in a region of the world in which no rescue is possible within any timeframe that matters, and in which the seafarers themselves constitute the only available emergency response. For Indians to reach this location under sail is, by any measure, a global first of meaningful weight — and one that the Indian state has now formally recognised through its peacetime gallantry framework.

The decision to honour these officers through the Shaurya Chakra rather than through a service medal or a civilian honour reflects a similar institutional choice to the one represented by the Nair Kirti Chakra: the elevation of frontier achievement to the same moral plane as gallantry under fire. It also has secondary significance for the contemporary positioning of women in the Indian Armed Forces. Navika Sagar Parikrama as a programme has been used by the Indian Navy as one of its most visible institutional vehicles for demonstrating the operational competence of women officers in demanding assignments. The Shaurya Chakras conferred on Monday formalise that demonstration into the gallantry record.

## The wider Shaurya Chakra picture

The other Shaurya Chakra citations on Monday returned the attention, in many cases, to the operational geography that has dominated such honours in recent years. Major Ashish Kumar of 7 Para (Special Forces) was honoured for the elimination of terrorists in Anantnag, in the Kashmir Valley. Constables Sanjay Tiwari and Feda Hussain Dar received the award for the neutralisation of a foreign terrorist in Srinagar, an operation during which both sustained injuries. Major Anshul Baltoo of the Jammu and Kashmir Light Infantry, attached to the 32 Assam Rifles, was recognised for a combing-and-search operation in the Dima Hasao district of southern Assam in April 2025, in which he confronted an armed cadre at close quarters and neutralised the individual single-handedly; the larger operation resulted in the elimination of three cadres and the recovery of weapons and war-like stores.

A separate Shaurya Chakra of distinct technical character was conferred on Wing Commander Abhimanyu Singh of the Indian Air Force, for the safe recovery of a fighter aircraft following a catastrophic mid-air canopy failure — the kind of citation that, like the Dilna K. and Roopa A. citation, sits at the intersection of operational skill and the courage required to apply that skill under extreme conditions. The aircraft and the lives aboard it were saved by a sequence of decisions made in flight; the citation recognises both the technical proficiency and the personal composure that made those decisions possible.

## The fifteen Vir Chakras

The fifteen Vir Chakras — twelve to living personnel, three posthumous — are the part of the ceremony that most clearly reflects the events of May 2025. The Vir Chakra is the third-highest wartime gallantry award of the Republic, after the Param Vir Chakra and the Maha Vir Chakra. It is conferred for acts of conspicuous bravery in the face of the enemy on land, at sea, or in the air. That fifteen such awards have been conferred at the June 2026 investiture ceremony reflects the operational record of Operation Sindoor, the four-day military exchange between India and Pakistan in May 2025 that followed the Pahalgam terror attack of April that year. The individual citations of these fifteen recipients have been read at the ceremony; their cumulative significance is that for the first time in many years, the Vir Chakra has been conferred in numbers reflective of a substantial military engagement — one that produced both visible victories and visible costs.

## The shape of the contemporary honour roll

Taken together, the seven Kirti Chakras, the fifteen Vir Chakras, and the twenty-nine Shaurya Chakras conferred on Monday produce a composite portrait of where Indian military and security personnel have, in the past several years, been asked to demonstrate the qualities the Republic chooses to honour. The Kashmir Valley and the Northeast remain the dominant theatres of close-quarter counter-terrorism, and the citations reflect this. Operation Sindoor has, for the first time in many years, brought wartime gallantry awards back into the investiture record at meaningful scale. The Indian Navy has been recognised for both anti-piracy operations in the Indian Ocean and for what might be called expeditionary courage in the polar latitudes. The Indian Air Force has been honoured both for combat and for the technical valour of aircraft recovery. And, in a deliberate institutional statement, the Republic has placed the preparation for human spaceflight within the same moral and honorific framework as conventional valour.

The Defence Investiture Ceremony-2026 (Phase I) on 8 June will be followed by a Phase II ceremony later in the year, at which additional recipients — including those whose awards are still being finalised — will be honoured. For now, what the Monday ceremony has produced is a public record of how the Republic of India, in the middle of 2026, defines and recognises the various forms of valour through which its uniformed personnel serve. The shape of that recognition, on close reading of the citations, is broader than it has been in some years past — a fact that, more than any single citation, may be the most significant analytical observation about Monday's ceremony.
